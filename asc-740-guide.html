ASC 740: Income Tax Accounting - A Practical Guide

Current vs. Deferred Tax  •  Temporary Differences  •  Valuation Allowances  •  Uncertain Tax Positions  •  Rate Reconciliation
ASC 740 ASC 740-10 (FIN 48) IRC §174A  •  IRC §168(k) Updated 2026
← cpavalidated.com

ASC 740 is the GAAP standard that governs how companies account for income taxes in their financial statements. It determines the income tax expense on the income statement and the deferred tax balances on the balance sheet. Every entity that prepares GAAP financial statements - public or private, large or small - must apply ASC 740. This guide explains the mechanics clearly, from first principles to the judgment-intensive areas where errors are most common.

What ASC 740 Is and Isn't

ASC 740 governs financial reporting of taxes - not the calculation of what you actually owe the IRS. The tax return (Form 1120, 1065, etc.) determines your cash tax liability. ASC 740 determines how that tax obligation, and the tax consequences of future events embedded in today's balance sheet, are presented in the financial statements. The two numbers - tax return tax and financial statement tax expense - are almost always different, sometimes dramatically so.

Temporary vs. Permanent Differences

The engine of deferred taxes is the concept of temporary differences - situations where GAAP and tax recognize income or expense in different periods. These differences will eventually reverse and produce future tax consequences. Permanent differences, by contrast, never reverse and produce no deferred tax.

Deferred Tax Assets and Liabilities

Once you identify your temporary differences, you calculate the deferred tax asset or liability by multiplying the temporary difference by the enacted tax rate expected to apply when the difference reverses. This is the liability method required by ASC 740.

Deferred Tax Calculation
Temporary DifferenceGAAP basis minus tax basis of asset or liability
×Enacted Tax RateRate expected to apply when difference reverses - not current year rate
=Deferred Tax Asset or LiabilityDTA if deductible temp diff; DTL if taxable temp diff
Example - Bonus Depreciation Creates a DTL
Asset cost (equipment placed in service 2026)$1,000,000
GAAP book basis (5-year straight-line, year 1)$800,000
Tax basis (100% bonus depreciation under OBBBA, IRC §168(k))$0
Taxable temporary difference (book basis exceeds tax basis)$800,000
Federal corporate rate (IRC §11)21%
Deferred tax liability recorded on balance sheet$168,000 DTL
Interpretation: company will owe $168K more tax in future years as book depreciation continues but no further tax deduction is available

A deferred tax liability arises when the tax basis of an asset is lower than its book basis (or a liability's tax basis is higher than book). You've deducted more for tax than for GAAP - you'll owe more tax later. Think bonus depreciation, installment sale gains, and accelerated amortization.

A deferred tax asset arises when the tax basis of an asset is higher than book (or a liability's tax basis is lower). You've recognized more for GAAP than you've deducted for tax - you'll pay less tax later. Think warranty accruals, bad debt reserves, and NOL carryforwards.

Valuation Allowances: The Most Judgmental Part of ASC 740

A deferred tax asset only has value if the company will generate enough future taxable income to actually use it. ASC 740-10-30-5 requires a valuation allowance against any DTA for which it is "more likely than not" - meaning greater than 50% probability - that some or all of the DTA will not be realized.

The four sources of taxable income that can support a DTA (listed in order of reliability under ASC 740-10-30-18) are:

1
Future reversals of existing taxable temporary differences
DTLs will reverse and create taxable income - this is the most objective source. A DTA can often be supported by DTLs scheduled to reverse in the same period and jurisdiction.
2
Future taxable income exclusive of reversals
Projected future profitability. This is subjective and requires persuasive evidence to rely upon, particularly when there is a history of losses. Projections must be based on feasible and supportable assumptions - not wishful thinking.
3
Taxable income in carryback years
If the jurisdiction allows carrybacks, prior year taxable income can absorb deductible temporary differences. Note: the CARES Act temporarily restored federal NOL carrybacks; current law limits federal NOL carryforwards to 80% of taxable income with no carryback (IRC §172 post-TCJA).
4
Tax planning strategies
Prudent and feasible strategies that management would implement to avoid allowing a carryforward to expire unused. These must be actions management would actually take - not hypothetical strategies invented solely to avoid a VA. Examples: accelerating income, deferring deductions, selling appreciated assets.
The "more likely than not" threshold is an asset-level test. You evaluate each DTA separately. A company can have a full VA against its NOL carryforward DTA while carrying a different DTA (e.g., for warranty reserves) without a VA, if there is sufficient objective evidence of future reversals to support the latter but not the former. The evaluation is granular, not all-or-nothing.
Cumulative losses are a strong presumption of VA requirement. Three years of cumulative pretax losses creates a rebuttable presumption that a VA is needed against the full DTA. Rebutting this presumption requires specific, persuasive evidence - not general optimism about future results. Auditors scrutinize VA analyses intensely when a company has a loss history.

Uncertain Tax Positions: ASC 740-10 (FIN 48)

Not every tax position on the return is certain to be sustained if challenged by the IRS or a state authority. ASC 740-10 (originally issued as FIN 48) requires companies to identify, measure, and disclose uncertain tax positions (UTPs) - any position where there is uncertainty about whether the tax treatment will be upheld.

Step 1: Recognition - The More-Likely-Than-Not Threshold

A tax benefit from an uncertain position may only be recognized in the financial statements if it is "more likely than not" - greater than 50% probability - that the position will be sustained on examination, based on its technical merits. If this threshold is not met, no benefit is recognized, regardless of how the position was reported on the tax return.

The evaluation is based on the technical merits of the position as if the taxing authority had full knowledge of all relevant facts. It ignores the probability of detection - a position is evaluated as if it will be audited.

Step 2: Measurement - Cumulative Probability

If the recognition threshold is met, the benefit is measured as the largest amount that is "more than 50% likely to be realized upon ultimate settlement." This is determined using a cumulative probability approach:

Measurement Example - Transfer Pricing Position
Possible outcome: full deduction sustained ($500K benefit)20% probability
Possible outcome: 80% of deduction sustained ($400K benefit)35% probability - cumulative 55%
Possible outcome: 60% of deduction sustained ($300K benefit)25% probability - cumulative 80%
Possible outcome: no deduction ($0 benefit)20% probability - cumulative 100%
Recognized benefit (largest amount with >50% cumulative probability)$400,000

Rollforward Disclosure

Public companies must disclose a tabular rollforward of the total amount of unrecognized tax benefits (UTBs) from the beginning to the end of the period, including increases for current-period positions, increases/decreases for prior-period positions, settlements, and lapse of statutes of limitations. Private companies have reduced disclosure requirements but must still identify and measure all UTPs.

The Rate Reconciliation

ASC 740 requires a reconciliation of the expected income tax expense at the statutory federal rate (21% for C-corporations) to the actual income tax expense reported. This reconciliation, disclosed in the financial statement footnotes, explains why the effective tax rate differs from 21%. It is one of the most scrutinized parts of the tax footnote.

Each line in the reconciliation tells a story. A large "change in valuation allowance" line signals uncertainty about future profitability. A large "state income taxes" line may reflect significant multistate operations. A large stock compensation line may indicate a highly valued option program. Analysts and auditors read the rate recon carefully - it is the bridge between the statutory rate and the company's actual tax burden.

OBBBA Impact on Tax Provisions (2026)

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (P.L. 119-21, signed July 4, 2025) introduced several provisions with significant ASC 740 implications that require immediate attention in 2026 provisions.

IRC §174A - Domestic R&D Expensing Restored

OBBBA enacted IRC §174A, which restores immediate expensing for domestic research and experimental (R&E) expenditures. Under the post-TCJA rule (pre-OBBBA), domestic R&E had to be capitalized and amortized over 5 years. For many R&D-intensive companies, this created a significant DTL - the tax deduction was slower than the GAAP expense.

OBBBA reverses this for domestic R&E incurred in tax years beginning after December 31, 2024. The DTL established for the TCJA-era capitalization is now reversed as those amounts become currently deductible. The DTL reversal timing must be carefully mapped to which R&E cohorts are affected. Foreign R&E remains subject to 15-year amortization under IRC §174.

IRC §168(k) - 100% Bonus Depreciation Permanent

OBBBA made 100% bonus depreciation permanent for qualifying property placed in service after January 19, 2025. The immediate ASC 740 impact: bonus depreciation creates DTLs, not DTAs. When an asset is fully deducted for tax in year 1 but depreciated over its GAAP useful life, the tax basis is zero while the book basis remains. This taxable temporary difference generates a DTL.

For companies with significant capital expenditure programs, permanent 100% bonus depreciation means a structural, recurring DTL that grows with each capital investment and reverses as book depreciation is recognized. Provision teams should update their fixed-asset rollforward templates to reflect the permanent nature of this rule rather than treating it as a phase-down that eventually disappears.

IRC §163(j) - EBITDA-Based Interest Limitation Restored

OBBBA permanently restored the EBITDA-based limitation for the §163(j) business interest deduction. Under TCJA (2018-2021), the limitation was based on EBITDA (allowing add-back of depreciation and amortization). TCJA shifted to an EBIT-based limitation for 2022-2025, which was more restrictive for capital-intensive businesses. OBBBA restored EBITDA permanently. Companies that had accumulated §163(j) disallowed interest carryforwards (a DTA) should reassess whether those carryforwards are more likely than not to be utilized under the more favorable EBITDA limitation.

NCTI (Formerly GILTI) - Outside Basis and Deferred Tax

OBBBA renamed GILTI as Net CFC Tested Income (NCTI) and eliminated the 10% QBAI exemption, while permanently setting the §250 deduction at 40%. For multinational companies: (1) the NCTI inclusion may differ from prior GILTI calculations requiring DTA/DTL updates on outside basis differences in CFC stock; (2) the indefinite reinvestment assertion (APB 23 / ASC 740-30) for outside basis differences must be reassessed given the NCTI changes; (3) the blended NCTI foreign tax credit rate affects the realizability of FTC carryforward DTAs.

Interim Period Reporting

For companies that report on a quarterly basis (all public companies; many private companies with quarterly reporting obligations), ASC 740-270 requires use of the Annual Effective Tax Rate (AETR) method.

The AETR method works as follows: at the start of each quarter, estimate the full-year pre-tax income and the full-year income tax expense. The ratio is the AETR. Apply the AETR to the year-to-date actual pre-tax income to compute year-to-date tax expense, then subtract the prior quarters' tax expense to get the current quarter expense.

Discrete items bypass the AETR. Certain items - called "discrete" - are recognized in the quarter they occur rather than being spread through the AETR. Common discrete items: changes in tax law enacted during the quarter (the OBBBA provisions, for example, were discrete items in Q3 2025); changes in valuation allowances related to a specific quarter's evidence; tax benefits from stock option exercises (ASC 718); settlements of UTPs; and prior period adjustments. Correctly identifying discrete versus ordinary items is one of the most common sources of interim provision error.

Balance Sheet Classification

Under ASC 740 (post-ASU 2015-17), all deferred tax assets and liabilities are classified as noncurrent on the balance sheet. There is no longer a current/noncurrent split for deferred taxes. DTAs and DTLs are netted within the same tax jurisdiction and presented as a single noncurrent DTA or DTL line item on the balance sheet. Amounts from different jurisdictions are not netted against each other.
