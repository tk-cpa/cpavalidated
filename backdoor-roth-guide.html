The Two Steps

1
Non-Deductible Traditional IRA Contribution
Contribute $7,000 ($8,000 if 50+) to a traditional IRA. No deduction taken. File Form 8606 Part I to establish basis. No income limit applies to contributions.
→
2
Convert to Roth
Convert the traditional IRA to a Roth IRA. If the only IRA funds are the just-contributed after-tax dollars (and no earnings accrued), the conversion is tax-free. IRC 408A(d)(3).
→
3
File Form 8606
Form 8606 Part II reports the conversion. If basis equals conversion amount, taxable amount = $0. Track basis permanently - it protects you.
v
Why this works. There is no income limit on contributing to a traditional IRA (it just may not be deductible). There is no income limit on converting a traditional IRA to a Roth. The backdoor Roth exploits both facts. The IRS is aware of this strategy. The step transaction doctrine has been considered but Congress has not acted to prohibit it. It is widely used and considered legitimate tax planning.

The Pro-Rata Rule - The #1 Trap

The Pro-Rata Rule - IRC 408(d)(2)

If you have ANY other pre-tax IRA money (traditional IRA, SEP IRA, SIMPLE IRA) anywhere, you cannot selectively convert only your non-deductible (after-tax) dollars. The IRS treats ALL your IRAs as one pool and applies a ratio.

Taxable % = (Pre-tax IRA balance) / (Total IRA balance including conversion)

Example: You have $93,000 in a pre-tax traditional IRA and contribute $7,000 non-deductible. Total IRA pool = $100,000. You convert $7,000 to Roth. Pro-rata: $93,000/$100,000 = 93% taxable. You pay tax on $6,510 of the $7,000 conversion. Only $490 is tax-free. The backdoor Roth barely works in this situation.

Sum of all pre-tax traditional IRA, SEP IRA, and SIMPLE IRA balances at December 31 of the conversion year. NOT Roth IRA balances.
The after-tax amount contributed and now converting. 2026 limit: $7,000 ($8,000 if 50+).

The Mega Backdoor Roth

i
A separate, larger strategy through a 401(k) plan. If your employer's 401(k) plan allows: (1) after-tax contributions above the regular $23,500 pre-tax limit, AND (2) in-plan Roth conversions or in-service withdrawals - you can contribute up to the Section 415 limit ($70,000 in 2026) and convert the after-tax portion to Roth. The after-tax non-Roth contribution can be up to ~$46,500 ($70,000 minus $23,500 employee + employer match). Not all plans allow this - check the plan document.
