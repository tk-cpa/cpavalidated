Business Sale Tax Planning: §338(h)(10), Asset vs. Stock & Installment Sales

IRC §338(h)(10)  •  §336(e)  •  Asset vs. Stock Analysis  •  §453 Installment Sales  •  Earnouts  •  Updated 2026
IRC §338(h)(10) IRC §453 IRC §1060 Updated 2026

The structure of a business sale determines who pays tax, how much, and when. A buyer who acquires stock inherits the selling company's tax history - its depreciation schedules, deferred income, and contingent liabilities. A buyer who acquires assets steps up their basis to fair market value immediately. These interests are usually in conflict: sellers prefer stock sales (one level of tax, capital gains rates), buyers prefer asset sales (full depreciation step-up). §338(h)(10) and §336(e) are elections that resolve this conflict by treating a stock sale as an asset sale for tax purposes - with the right facts, both sides can get what they want.

Why Structure Determines Everything

Consider a C-corporation with $10M of assets with a tax basis of $1M, sold for $10M. Stock sale: Shareholders pay tax on $10M - $1M (their stock basis) = $9M gain at 23.8% (20% + 3.8% NIIT) = $2.14M tax. Buyer gets no step-up - must depreciate old basis going forward. Asset sale: Corporation pays tax on $9M gain; if all ordinary recapture, 21% corporate rate = $1.89M. Then shareholders pay tax on dividend distribution of remaining $8.11M. Total two-level tax can exceed $4M. The structure gap between buyer and seller is real and negotiable.

Asset Sale vs. Stock Sale: The Core Comparison

Asset Sale
Stock Sale
Buyer perspective
Gets full step-up in basis of all acquired assets to fair market value. Immediately depreciates (with bonus depreciation at 100%) tangible assets. Amortizes goodwill and intangibles over 15 years under §197. No hidden tax liabilities inherited.
Buyer perspective
Gets basis equal to purchase price in the stock only - no step-up in underlying assets. Inherits all historical tax liabilities, deferred taxes, and contingent liabilities of the target. Must continue existing depreciation schedules.
Seller perspective (C-corp)
Two levels of tax: corporate-level gain on asset sale (ordinary rates on recapture, capital rates on §1231 gain), then shareholder-level tax on liquidating distribution. Most expensive structure for C-corp sellers.
Seller perspective (C-corp)
One level of tax: shareholders pay capital gains tax on stock sale. No corporate-level tax. Most favorable for C-corp sellers. Buyer disfavors this.
Seller perspective (S-corp / partnership)
Generally one level of tax (pass-through). Ordinary income on recapture (§1245, §1250, §751 hot assets); long-term capital gain on §1231 assets and goodwill. Seller may prefer stock to avoid ordinary recapture.
Seller perspective (S-corp / partnership)
Generally one level of tax. Capital gain rates on entire gain (with some exceptions for §751 hot assets in partnerships). Sellers typically prefer stock sales.

IRC §338(h)(10): The Bridge Election

§338(h)(10) is an election available when a buyer acquires 80% or more of the stock of an S-corporation or a subsidiary in a consolidated group. The election treats the transaction as an asset purchase for tax purposes even though it is legally a stock purchase. The target is deemed to sell all of its assets at fair market value (generating asset-level gain/loss), then liquidate tax-free into the parent.

Who benefits: The buyer gets the full asset step-up (same as an asset purchase), including 100% bonus depreciation on qualifying tangible assets and 15-year §197 amortization on goodwill. The seller gets simplified documentation and potentially avoids the practical complications of an actual asset transfer (re-titling assets, consents, novations of contracts).

The cost: The seller recognizes gain at the asset level rather than the stock level. For S-corporation sellers, this is generally pass-through gain to shareholders at capital gains rates - similar to a direct asset sale. The §338(h)(10) election is most commonly used in S-corporation acquisitions because it aligns buyer and seller interests without a significant additional tax cost to the seller.

§338(h)(10) requires mutual agreement. Both buyer and seller must jointly make the election on Form 8023. Neither party can make the election unilaterally. This creates negotiating leverage - a buyer willing to pay a higher price in exchange for the seller's agreement to a §338(h)(10) election is a common deal dynamic, where the buyer's tax value of the step-up exceeds the seller's incremental tax cost.

IRC §336(e): The Broader Alternative

§336(e) is similar to §338(h)(10) but broader. It applies to sales of S-corporation stock by any seller (not just consolidated group transactions) and to distributions of subsidiary stock by a parent. It can be made unilaterally by the selling party - no buyer consent required. The tax treatment is similar: deemed asset sale at fair market value, generating gain at the entity level.

The §336(e) election is particularly useful in S-corporation sales where the buyer is unwilling to jointly file Form 8023 (perhaps because they want to preserve target attributes) but the seller wants to offer an asset purchase structure to attract better pricing from buyers who value the step-up.

Purchase Price Allocation: IRC §1060

In any asset acquisition (actual or deemed under §338(h)(10)/(336(e))), the purchase price must be allocated among the acquired assets using the residual method under IRC §1060. Assets are allocated in a specific order (the "7 classes"), with any excess allocated last to goodwill and going concern value:

ClassAsset TypeTax Character
ICash and general deposit accountsOrdinary (no gain)
IIActively traded personal property, CDs, foreign currencyCapital/ordinary depending on asset
IIIMark-to-market assets, debt instrumentsOrdinary income (accounts receivable, etc.)
IVInventory and stock in tradeOrdinary income
VAll other assets (equipment, real property)§1231 gain (capital) + §1245/1250 recapture (ordinary)
VI§197 intangibles except goodwill and going concernOrdinary income on recapture; capital on excess
VIIGoodwill and going concern valueCapital gain - most favorable to sellers

Buyers want to allocate more to Class V assets (fully depreciable with bonus depreciation) and less to goodwill. Sellers want to allocate more to goodwill (capital gains rates) and less to accounts receivable and inventory (ordinary income). The §1060 allocation must be consistent between buyer and seller and reported on Form 8594.

Installment Sales: IRC §453

An installment sale allows the seller to recognize gain proportionally as payments are received, rather than in the year of sale. The installment method applies automatically unless the seller elects out. Each payment received is divided into return of basis, gain, and interest using the gross profit ratio.

Strategic uses of installment sales. Spreading gain over multiple years keeps the seller's AGI below NIIT thresholds in each year, may keep capital gains in lower rate brackets, and defers the tax bill. For business owners with concentrated wealth, the installment sale can be combined with a Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT) or other structure. The downside: the seller bears credit risk on the buyer's obligation, and interest must be imputed at the AFR if not specified in the agreement.

The installment method is not available for publicly traded securities (sold on an established market), inventory, or recapture income under §1245 and §1250 - recapture must be recognized in full in the year of sale regardless of when payments are received. This creates a mismatch: the ordinary recapture income is recognized upfront while the capital gain can be spread.

Earnouts: Tax Treatment

Earnouts - contingent payments tied to post-closing performance - are common in business sales. Their tax treatment depends on structure. If the earnout meets the requirements for installment reporting (the amount is determinable using a fixed formula), it can be reported on the installment method. If the amount is entirely contingent (not determinable), the seller either: (a) uses the open transaction doctrine (rare, requires IRS approval) and recognizes basis first before any gain, or (b) treats payments as gain when received with basis allocated ratably over the earnout period.

Buyer deduction timing for earnout payments is separate from seller income recognition. If the earnout is treated as contingent purchase price, the buyer capitalizes payments as additional cost basis when paid. If the earnout is treated as compensation (common when sellers are also employees post-closing), the buyer deducts it currently but the seller pays ordinary income tax rather than capital gains rates. Distinguishing price from compensation is a frequent IRS audit focus in business acquisitions.
