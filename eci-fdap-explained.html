ECI vs FDAP: How the US Taxes Foreign Persons

Effectively connected income  •  FDAP at 30%  •  The §871(d) election  •  Branch profits tax
← Cross-border content

When the US taxes a foreign person, it does it one of two ways. The first is graduated rates on net income (after deductions), the way a US person is taxed - this is for income "effectively connected with a US trade or business." The second is a flat 30% on gross income (no deductions) - this is for passive income that flows out of the US. The two regimes look different, behave differently, and require different planning. Knowing which bucket each piece of income falls into is the central question for non-residents and foreign corporations doing business with or in the US.

The One-Sentence Summary

ECI is taxed at graduated rates on net income (after deductions). FDAP is taxed at 30% on gross income (no deductions, with treaty reductions available). The classification depends on whether the income arises from a US trade or business or is passive US-source income flowing to a non-resident.

What Counts as ECI

"Effectively connected" means connected to a US trade or business. The IRC §864(c) framework looks at two questions:

  1. Is the foreign person engaged in a US trade or business? This is a factual test - regular, continuous, and considerable activity in the US. Selling a single property is generally not a trade or business. Operating a US store, factory, office, or service-delivery operation is.
  2. If yes, what income is effectively connected to it? US-source income from the activity is generally ECI. Foreign-source income usually isn't (with narrow exceptions). Investment income held by the trade or business may or may not be ECI depending on the asset use test and business activities test.

What's Always ECI

  • Wages and salaries from US employment - automatically ECI for the non-resident employee
  • Income from selling US real estate - made ECI by IRC §897 (FIRPTA), regardless of whether the seller is otherwise in a US trade or business
  • Personal services performed in the US by an independent contractor (self-employment) - ECI from the US activity
  • Net rental income on US real estate when the §871(d) election is made (more on this below)

What's Sometimes ECI

Investment income held by a foreign person engaged in a US trade or business may be ECI if either:

  • Asset use test - the asset is used in the US trade or business (working capital, inventory)
  • Business activities test - the activities of the trade or business were a material factor in producing the income

For example, a foreign corporation operating a US restaurant chain holds excess cash in interest-bearing accounts. The interest is ECI if the cash is working capital for the restaurant business, but FDAP if it's an unrelated investment portfolio.

What Counts as FDAP

FDAP is the catch-all category for passive US-source income flowing to non-residents. The classic types:

  • Dividends from US corporations
  • Interest on US obligations (with the major exception of "portfolio interest" - see below)
  • Royalties for use of US property (patents, copyrights, trademarks)
  • Rents on US real property (without the §871(d) election)
  • Pensions and annuities from US sources (treaty often modifies)
  • Compensation for services performed in the US when not ECI (rare)
  • Original issue discount, certain swap payments, gambling winnings

What's NOT FDAP

Several categories of US-source income flowing to non-residents are exempt from the 30% withholding regime:

  • Portfolio interest. Interest on most registered US debt obligations is exempt under IRC §871(h) and §881(c). This covers most corporate bonds and Treasury securities held by non-residents. The exemption requires the recipient be a non-related party and provide a Form W-8BEN.
  • Bank deposit interest. Interest on US bank deposits is generally exempt from the 30% withholding for non-residents. Reported on Form 1042-S informationally but not taxed.
  • Capital gains on personal property. Generally not US-source for non-residents (except FIRPTA real property gains). A non-resident who sells US stock at a gain typically owes no US tax on the gain, even though the issuer is American.
  • Income covered by a treaty exemption. Royalties from non-resident X to US payer Y are FDAP; if a treaty between X's country and the US reduces the rate to 0%, the FDAP rate becomes 0%.

The Net Rental Election Under §871(d)

This is one of the most important elections for non-resident owners of US real estate. Without the election, US rental income is FDAP - 30% withholding on gross rents, with no deductions for property taxes, mortgage interest, depreciation, repairs, or management fees.

Why this matters. A Canadian owns a Florida rental property generating $24,000 of annual gross rent. Property taxes, mortgage interest, insurance, and management fees total $20,000, leaving $4,000 of net economic income. Without the §871(d) election: 30% of $24,000 = $7,200 of US tax (withheld). The owner pays more US tax than they earn in net income. With the §871(d) election: graduated rates on $4,000 of net income - typically 10% to 12% bracket = roughly $400 to $480 of US tax. The election saves more than $6,500 a year on a single rental property.

The election is made by attaching a statement to the first Form 1040-NR for which it applies. Once made, the rental income is ECI - graduated rates on net rental income, with deductions for ordinary and necessary expenses including depreciation. The election applies to all US real property of the taxpayer and continues until revoked (with IRS consent).

Practical sequence for a foreign rental property owner:

  1. Obtain an ITIN if you don't already have an SSN (Form W-7)
  2. Complete a W-8ECI for the property manager or tenant, claiming ECI status (this stops 30% withholding at source)
  3. Make the §871(d) election on the first applicable Form 1040-NR
  4. File 1040-NR annually with Schedule E (rental income and expenses)
Don't skip the W-8ECI. If the property manager has only a W-9 (or nothing) on file, they will withhold 30% of gross rents under FDAP rules, regardless of the owner's intent to make the §871(d) election. The election retroactively converts the rent to ECI, but the over-withholding has to be reclaimed via the 1040-NR refund - tying up cash for 12-18 months.

The Corporate Equivalents

For foreign corporations, the rules parallel the individual rules but with corporate sections:

  • IRC §882 - taxes foreign corporations on ECI at the corporate graduated rate (currently 21%). Same definition of ECI as for individuals.
  • IRC §881 - imposes a 30% withholding on FDAP-type income paid to foreign corporations. Same exemptions (portfolio interest, bank deposits) apply.
  • IRC §884 - the branch profits tax. An additional 30% tax (or treaty rate) on the deemed dividend equivalent amount of a foreign corporation's US branch earnings, designed to put a foreign corporation operating through a branch on rough parity with one operating through a US subsidiary that pays dividends.

The Branch Profits Tax

If a foreign corporation operates a US branch, the branch's ECI is taxed at 21% under §882. But once those earnings are conceptually "repatriated" to the foreign corporation, an additional layer of tax applies: the branch profits tax under §884.

The mechanics: each year, you compute the "dividend equivalent amount" - roughly, the after-tax ECI not reinvested in US assets. That amount is treated as if it were a dividend from a US subsidiary, subject to the 30% rate (or lower treaty rate, often 5% to 15%). The result: total US tax on a foreign corporation's branch operations is approximately 21% + (effective branch profits rate * (1 - 21%)) = roughly mid-30s percent without a treaty, mid-20s with a typical treaty.

The branch profits tax is the reason most foreign corporations doing significant business in the US prefer to operate through a US subsidiary (corporation) rather than a branch. The subsidiary structure gives clean deferral of the second-tier tax until actual dividends, and clearer treaty access.

Treaty Rate Reductions on FDAP

The 30% statutory FDAP rate is the starting point. Tax treaties between the US and other countries often reduce or eliminate it for residents of the treaty country.

Typical treaty reductions:

  • Dividends - usually reduced to 5% or 15% (5% for substantial corporate ownership, 15% otherwise). The US-Canada treaty Article X is the classic example.
  • Interest - often 0% or 10%. The US-Canada treaty has 0% on most interest.
  • Royalties - usually 0% or 10%.
  • Pensions and annuities - often only taxed by the country of residence.

To claim the treaty rate, the recipient submits Form W-8BEN (for individuals) or W-8BEN-E (for entities) to the US payer, certifying treaty residence and the article being relied on. The payer then withholds at the reduced rate instead of 30%.

Putting It Together: A Common Pattern

A Canadian individual owns:

  • A US brokerage account holding US stocks paying dividends
  • A US bank account paying interest
  • A Florida rental property
  • No US business activities

Tax treatment:

  • Dividends from US stocks - FDAP. 30% statutory rate, reduced to 15% under US-Canada treaty Article X. Withheld at source by the broker (W-8BEN on file). No 1040-NR required for dividends alone.
  • Interest on US bank deposits - FDAP, but exempt from withholding under the bank-deposit exception.
  • Capital gains on US stocks - generally not US-source for non-residents. No US tax.
  • Florida rental income - FDAP at 30% on gross unless §871(d) election made. With election, becomes ECI taxed at graduated rates on net. Annual 1040-NR required.

A typical Canadian Florida rental owner files Form 1040-NR each year reporting Schedule E rental income and the §871(d)-elected ECI treatment, attaches a treaty position via W-8BEN with the broker for dividend rate reduction, and otherwise has no US tax obligation. When the rental property is eventually sold, FIRPTA withholding kicks in - see the FIRPTA article for those mechanics.

Practical Recommendations

Identify each income stream's bucket. Before structuring or entering a US activity, classify the expected income into ECI or FDAP. This drives the after-tax economics in opposite directions.

Make the §871(d) election early if you own US rental property. The election can be retroactive in some cases, but the cleaner path is to make it the first year and have the property manager use a W-8ECI from day one.

Use treaty rates where available. A correctly executed W-8BEN claiming treaty benefits can cut withholding from 30% to 15% (dividends), 0% (most interest), or 0% (most royalties for arm's-length payers).

For corporate operations, consider the structure carefully. A US branch of a foreign corporation triggers the branch profits tax. A US subsidiary (US corporation owned by the foreign parent) generally produces a cleaner structure with treaty-based withholding on actual dividends. The right structure depends on operations, treaty access, and exit strategy.

Don't ignore FBAR and Form 8938 on the home-country side. A foreign person with US activities is also a person with home-country reporting on US accounts - and often vice versa. The reporting symmetry catches people up after the income tax planning is settled.
