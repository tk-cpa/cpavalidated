Energy Tax Credits: §30D EV Credit, §25C Home Energy & §48 Commercial

The Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 overhauled and significantly expanded the energy tax credit landscape. New credits, higher limits, transferability to third parties, and direct pay options transformed energy credits from niche planning items into mainstream considerations for individuals buying vehicles, upgrading homes, and businesses investing in clean energy infrastructure. This guide covers the three main individual and business energy credits and their current status.

OBBBA Status - Energy Credits Remain in Effect

OBBBA P.L. 119-21 did not repeal the IRA energy credits. The §30D clean vehicle credit, §25C home energy improvement credit, and §48 commercial investment tax credit all remain in effect for 2026. Some credits have scheduled phase-down dates. Verify eligibility for the specific year before purchasing - these credits have changed frequently and may change again.

§30D Clean Vehicle Credit: New Electric Vehicles

IRC §30D provides a credit of up to $7,500 for new clean vehicles (fully electric, plug-in hybrid, or fuel cell) placed in service after December 31, 2022. The credit has two components of $3,750 each: one for North American final assembly and one for battery component sourcing from North America or free-trade-agreement countries. A vehicle qualifies for the full $7,500 only if both tests are met; partial credit ($3,750) if only one is met.

Income Limits (MAGI)

  • Single / HOH: $150,000 MAGI limit
  • Married filing jointly: $300,000 MAGI limit
  • Married filing separately: $150,000 MAGI limit

MAGI is measured in either the year of purchase or the prior year - whichever is lower. This allows a taxpayer who anticipates exceeding the limit in the current year to use the prior year's lower MAGI for eligibility.

Vehicle Price Limits

  • Vans, SUVs, and pickup trucks: $80,000 MSRP cap
  • All other vehicles (sedans, cars): $55,000 MSRP cap

Point-of-Sale Transfer (Since 2024)

The IRA allowed taxpayers to transfer the §30D credit to the dealer at point of sale starting January 1, 2024, receiving the credit as a direct price reduction on the vehicle. This is significant because the credit is now usable even by taxpayers with no federal tax liability. The dealer advances the credit and is reimbursed by the IRS.

§25E Used Clean Vehicle Credit

A credit of 30% of the purchase price, up to $4,000, for used clean vehicles purchased from a dealer (not a private sale). The vehicle must be at least 2 model years old at time of sale. Income limits: $75,000 (single), $150,000 (MFJ). Vehicle price cannot exceed $25,000. No more than one used vehicle credit per taxpayer per 3-year period.

§25C Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit

The IRA overhauled §25C into a 30% credit on qualifying home energy improvements with an annual cap of $3,200 (replacing the old $500 lifetime cap). The credit is nonrefundable and applies to a principal residence in the US. Qualifying improvements and their sub-limits:

§25D Residential Clean Energy Credit

A separate credit (often confused with §25C) for solar panels, solar water heaters, wind turbines, geothermal heat pumps, fuel cells, and battery storage installed at a personal residence. Rate is 30% through 2032, then phases down to 26% in 2033 and 22% in 2034. No annual dollar cap - the credit is 30% of the full installation cost. Excess credit carries forward to future years.

§48 Investment Tax Credit: Commercial and Business Energy

The §48 ITC applies to solar, wind, fuel cell, and other qualifying energy property placed in service in a trade or business. The base credit rate is 6%, increased to 30% for projects meeting prevailing wage and apprenticeship requirements (or for projects under 1 MW). The IRA also added a "bonus" credit for projects in energy communities and low-income communities. The §48C advanced manufacturing credit provides additional credits for qualifying manufacturing facilities.

Transferability for businesses (IRC §6418). The IRA created the ability to transfer (sell) certain business clean energy credits to unrelated third parties. A business with large §48 credits but insufficient tax liability to use them can sell the credits for cash to a tax-equity buyer. This is a major change that makes clean energy investment economically viable for loss-stage companies, nonprofits electing direct pay, and any entity that cannot use the credits directly.
