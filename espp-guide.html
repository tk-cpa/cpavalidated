ESPP Tax Treatment: Qualifying vs. Disqualifying Dispositions

§423 Plan Rules • 15% Discount • Qualifying Disposition • Ordinary Income vs. Capital Gain
IRC §423Form 3922Treas. Reg. §1.423-2
← Investments & Capital

Employee Stock Purchase Plans (ESPPs) allow employees to buy company stock at a discount - typically 15% below market price. The tax treatment depends entirely on whether the shares are held long enough to produce a qualifying disposition. Get it right and most of the gain is long-term capital gain. Get it wrong - or simply not know the rules - and the entire discount plus appreciation becomes ordinary income on the W-2 in the year of sale.

The Two Outcomes

Qualifying disposition: Held more than 2 years from offering date AND more than 1 year from purchase date. Ordinary income is limited to the lesser of: (a) the actual discount received (offering price discount), or (b) the gain on sale. The remainder is long-term capital gain. Best possible tax outcome.

Disqualifying disposition: Sold or transferred before either holding period is met. The entire bargain element at purchase (FMV on purchase date minus purchase price) is ordinary income in the year of sale, regardless of what the stock is worth at sale. Added to W-2. Subject to FICA.

How the §423 Plan Works

Under a qualified §423 ESPP, the employer sets an offering period (typically 6 or 24 months) during which employees contribute payroll deductions. At the end of the period, the plan purchases shares at a discount - usually 15% off the lower of the stock price at the start or end of the offering period. This "look-back" feature means employees benefit from any increase during the offering period plus the guaranteed 15% discount.

The offering date (start of the offering period) and the purchase date (when shares are bought) are both critical. The 2-year clock runs from the offering date. The 1-year clock runs from the purchase date. Both must be satisfied for qualifying disposition treatment.

Qualifying Disposition: The Tax Math

Example: Offering price $100, purchase price $85 (15% discount), sale price $130 after holding both periods. Ordinary income = lesser of (a) $15 discount or (b) $45 gain = $15. The remaining $45 gain less $15 = $30 is long-term capital gain. Basis = $85 purchase price + $15 ordinary income = $100. Long-term gain = $130 - $100 = $30.

The qualifying disposition tax outcome is excellent. The only ordinary income is the original discount (capped at the actual gain if the stock declined). All appreciation above that is long-term capital gain. For employees who can hold the shares for two years from offering, this is one of the most tax-efficient forms of compensation available.

Disqualifying Disposition: The Cost

If shares are sold before either holding period, the bargain element on the purchase date - the full FMV on purchase date minus the purchase price - is W-2 ordinary income in the year of sale. The employer adds it to Box 1. FICA applies. The employee's basis in the stock is then FMV on purchase date, so any further appreciation or loss from purchase date to sale date is capital gain or loss (with the holding period starting on the purchase date).

Many employees inadvertently trigger disqualifying dispositions without realizing it. A common scenario: employee sells ESPP shares within 12 months of purchase to fund a down payment or cover an expense. The entire bargain element - which can be 15% plus the offering period appreciation - becomes W-2 income. The tax bill arrives via the W-2 at year-end, sometimes surprising employees who thought they were just selling stock at a gain. Know the purchase date and the offering date before selling.
