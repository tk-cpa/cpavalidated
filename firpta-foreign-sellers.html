FIRPTA: Selling US Real Estate as a Foreign Person

15% / 10% / 0% tiers  •  Form 8288  •  Withholding certificate  •  Refund process
← Canadian Tax Hub

When a foreign person sells US real estate, the IRS doesn't trust them to file a tax return and pay what they owe later. So the law forces the buyer to take a chunk of the sales price and send it directly to the IRS at closing. That chunk is up to 15% of the gross sale price - not the profit, the whole price. The seller gets the difference back later by filing a US tax return. This is FIRPTA, and the rules around it have several moving parts that catch unprepared sellers off guard.

The One-Sentence Summary

If you are a foreign person selling a US real property interest, the buyer must withhold up to 15% of the gross sale price at closing, file Form 8288 within 20 days, and send the withheld amount to the IRS. You then file a US tax return showing your actual gain to recover the excess as a refund. There are exceptions and ways to reduce the withholding upfront - but you have to act before closing.

Why FIRPTA Exists

Until 1980, foreign investors could buy US real estate, sell at a profit, and walk away without paying any US tax - because the IRS had no practical way to enforce against someone outside the country. Congress fixed this with the Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act, which did two things:

  1. Made the gain on sale of US real estate by a foreign person taxable as US business income (ECI), regardless of whether the seller was otherwise engaged in a US business.
  2. Created the FIRPTA withholding mechanism to actually collect the tax at closing, with the buyer responsible for getting it to the IRS.

The key thing to understand: FIRPTA isn't a separate tax. It's a withholding mechanism for tax that's already owed under regular US tax rules. The 15% (or 10%, or 0%) is just a prepayment. Your actual US tax is calculated on your actual gain when you file the return.

The Three FIRPTA Tiers

The withholding rate depends on the sale price and the buyer's intended use of the property.

Withholding When It Applies
0% Sale price $300,000 or less, AND the buyer is an individual who will use the property as a residence (50% or more of days used in each of the first two 12-month periods after closing). The buyer signs a "personal residence affidavit" stating their intent.
10% Sale price $300,001 to $1,000,000, AND the buyer is an individual who will use the property as a residence (same 50% test, same affidavit).
15% Default. Sale price over $1,000,000, OR the buyer is not an individual (corporation, LLC, partnership, trust), OR the buyer doesn't intend to use it as a residence (investment, rental, second home as investment, vacant land, etc.).
How the math works. A Canadian seller is selling a Florida condo to a US individual buyer who plans to live in it as a primary residence. Sale price: $850,000. Because the price is between $300,000 and $1,000,000 and the buyer is an individual using it as a residence, the FIRPTA withholding rate is 10%. Withholding = $85,000. That $85,000 is taken from the seller's net proceeds at closing and sent to the IRS within 20 days. The seller's actual capital gains tax (assuming, say, $200,000 of gain at 15% federal long-term rate) might only be $30,000 - so the seller is overpaid by $55,000 and will file Form 1040-NR to claim that as a refund.

Defining "Foreign Person" and "US Real Property Interest"

Foreign Person

For FIRPTA purposes, a "foreign person" includes:

  • Non-resident alien individuals
  • Foreign corporations (without an effective §897(i) election)
  • Foreign partnerships, foreign trusts, foreign estates

A US resident alien is not a foreign person, even though they're not a citizen. A green card holder selling US real estate is treated as a US person for FIRPTA - no withholding required if the buyer obtains a non-foreign affidavit.

US Real Property Interest (USRPI)

The most common USRPIs:

  • Direct ownership of US land and buildings (residential or commercial)
  • Improvements and structures on US land
  • Personal property associated with US real property (furniture in a furnished sale, fixtures, equipment)
  • Stock in a "United States real property holding corporation" (USRPHC) - a US corporation more than 50% of whose assets are USRPIs
  • Effective January 2026, certain partnership interests in partnerships holding USRPIs or conducting US business (under IRC §1446(f)) - subject to a separate 10% withholding rate

Form 8288-B: Reducing the Withholding Upfront

The 15% (or 10%) is gross withholding - calculated on the sales price, not the gain. For sellers with little or no actual gain, the withholding is dramatically larger than the tax owed.

The fix: Form 8288-B, Application for Withholding Certificate. The seller (or the buyer) submits this to the IRS before or at the time of sale, asking the IRS to authorize a reduced withholding amount based on the actual expected tax liability.

The math that justifies a withholding certificate. If your gain is small and your expected tax is far below 15% of the gross price, Form 8288-B is the only way to keep the cash from being held by the IRS for many months while you wait for a refund. A loss sale, in particular, should always include a withholding certificate application - there is no actual tax owed, and the IRS can authorize 0% withholding if the application supports it.

What the Application Includes

  • The seller's name, address, and US taxpayer identification number (ITIN, applied for if not held)
  • Property description and sale price
  • Calculation of the gain (sale price minus basis minus selling costs)
  • Calculation of the expected federal tax
  • Supporting documentation: original purchase HUD-1 or settlement statement, capital improvement records, current sale contract

Timing

The IRS officially has 90 days to act on a Form 8288-B application. In practice, IRS processing has been running longer for several years - 4 to 6 months is common.

If the application is filed before or at closing, the buyer is allowed to hold the 15%/10% in escrow rather than remit it to the IRS at the 20-day mark. When the IRS issues the withholding certificate, the lower amount is then remitted (within 20 days of the certificate's issuance) and the rest is released to the seller. If the IRS denies the application, the full amount is remitted.

If the certificate application is filed after closing, the buyer must still withhold and remit the full amount within 20 days of the closing. The escrow benefit only works when the application is timely. After closing, the only path to recover excess withholding is the regular refund process via Form 1040-NR, which takes 6-12 months in most cases.

Form 8288 and the Buyer's 20-Day Deadline

The buyer is the withholding agent. The buyer (or a "qualified substitute" - typically the title company or closing attorney) is responsible for:

  1. Determining whether FIRPTA applies - including obtaining a non-foreign affidavit from the seller if the seller claims to be a US person
  2. Calculating the correct withholding rate based on the tier system
  3. Withholding the amount at closing
  4. Filing Form 8288 (US Withholding Tax Return for Dispositions by Foreign Persons of US Real Property Interests) with Form 8288-A (Statement of Withholding) within 20 days of the transfer date
  5. Remitting the withholding via EFTPS - paper checks no longer accepted
EFTPS is now mandatory. Effective September 30, 2025, FIRPTA withholding payments must be made electronically via EFTPS. This is a change from the prior paper-check practice. Buyers and closing agents involved in FIRPTA transactions should register for EFTPS before closing to avoid 20-day deadline pressure on the back end.

What Happens If the Buyer Misses the Deadline

The buyer is personally liable for the full withholding amount, plus penalties and interest, if they fail to withhold or remit timely. Failure-to-file penalties under IRC §6651 are 5% per month up to 25% of the unpaid tax, and willful failure to collect or pay can trigger penalties under IRC §7202 of up to $10,000.

This is why most title companies in states with significant foreign-buyer activity (Florida, California, New York, Texas) have FIRPTA compliance built into their standard closing checklist. If you're a buyer of property from a foreign seller, do not assume your title company is handling FIRPTA - confirm in writing.

Refund Process: Form 1040-NR

Even after FIRPTA withholding has been remitted, the foreign seller almost always has tax due (or a refund coming) on the actual transaction. The mechanism: file Form 1040-NR (for individuals) or Form 1120-F (for corporations) for the year of sale, reporting the gain on Schedule D, applying any treaty benefits, and claiming the FIRPTA withholding as a payment via attached Copy B of Form 8288-A.

Filing Mechanics

  • Form 1040-NR for individuals. Due date: June 15 if no US wages and no permanent residence in the US (April 15 in other cases). Six-month extension available via Form 4868.
  • Form 1120-F for foreign corporations. Due date: generally the 15th day of the 6th month following the corporate fiscal year-end.
  • ITIN required. Foreign individual sellers without an SSN must apply for an ITIN via Form W-7. There is a specific FIRPTA exception in the W-7 instructions (Exception 4) that allows the ITIN application to be filed without the usual requirement to attach a tax return.
  • Attach Form 8288-A (Copy B) showing the withholding. Without this attachment, the IRS will not credit the withholding against the seller's tax.

Refund Timing

Foreign-seller FIRPTA refunds historically run 6 to 12 months. Returns filed early in the calendar year tend to process faster than those filed close to the extended deadline. Refunds can be direct-deposited to a US bank account or sent by check to the seller's foreign address.

Treaty Effects and State-Level Withholding

Treaty Considerations

The US-Canada treaty does not eliminate FIRPTA withholding - both countries retain the right to tax gains on real property situated within their territory (Article XIII). The treaty does enable the FTC mechanism, so Canadian sellers can credit the US tax against Canadian tax on the same gain.

Some other treaties (with countries that do not tax capital gains on real estate or that allocate the gain entirely to the country of residence) can produce more favorable outcomes - those positions need to be claimed on Form 8833 with the 1040-NR.

State Withholding

FIRPTA is a federal regime. Several states impose their own nonresident-seller withholding on top of FIRPTA, with separate forms and deadlines:

  • California: 3.33% withholding on gross price for non-resident sellers (Form 593)
  • New York: Estimated tax payment required for non-resident sellers (Form IT-2663)
  • Hawaii (HARPTA): 7.25% withholding on gross price
  • Maryland, Georgia, North Carolina: All have their own non-resident withholding

Florida, Texas, Washington, Nevada, and other no-income-tax states have no parallel state withholding, which is one reason these markets attract foreign real estate investors.

Practical Recommendations

Apply for the withholding certificate before closing. If your gain is modest or you have a loss, Form 8288-B saves significant time-value-of-money. Don't wait until after closing.

Get the ITIN process started early. If the seller doesn't have an SSN or ITIN, the application can take 8-10 weeks. Without an ITIN, the withholding certificate application gets delayed and the refund process is blocked.

Don't rely on the buyer's affidavit alone. If the buyer claims to be using the property as a residence (to qualify for the 0% or 10% tier), the seller has no recourse if the buyer's representation is false - the IRS can reassess the buyer for the full 15% if the residence requirement isn't actually met. Make sure the affidavit is properly documented and the buyer is aware of the 50%-of-days requirement for the first two years.

For partnership interest sales, watch for IRC §1446(f). The January 2026 Form 8288 expansion brought partnership interest transfers under the same form structure, with a 10% withholding rate. If you're a foreign partner selling out, the buyer's withholding obligation is now on Form 8288 Part III.

Plan US estate exposure separately. FIRPTA handles the income tax on sale. US estate tax on US-situs real property held at death is a separate problem with a $60,000 unified-credit-equivalent for non-residents - far below the US-citizen threshold. Cross-border estate planning often involves holding US real property through a Canadian or other foreign holding company structure to mitigate this.
