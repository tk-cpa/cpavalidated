Form 5472: Foreign-Owned US Corporations & Reporting

Who Must File  •  Reportable Transactions  •  $25,000 Penalty  •  Foreign-Owned SMLLCs  •  Related Party Rules  •  Updated 2026
IRC §6038A IRC §6038C Treas. Reg. §1.6038A
← International Tax

Form 5472 is one of the most heavily penalized information returns in the US tax code - $25,000 per form per year, automatically, with no showing of harm required. It applies to 25%-or-more foreign-owned US corporations and, since 2017, to foreign-owned single-member LLCs that are disregarded entities. Many foreign entrepreneurs operating through US entities have no idea the form exists until the IRS assesses the penalty. This guide covers who files, what gets reported, and how the penalty works.

The $25,000 Penalty - No Harm Required

IRC §6038A(d) imposes a $25,000 penalty for each failure to file a required Form 5472, each failure to maintain required records, and each failure to provide information when requested by the IRS. The penalty applies per form per tax year - a company with 3 foreign related parties that fails to file for 3 years owes $225,000 in penalties before any tax is assessed. The penalty is not discretionary and does not require the IRS to show any tax underpayment. It is purely an information reporting penalty.

Who Must File: The Two Categories

Category 1: 25% Foreign-Owned Domestic Corporations

A US corporation (including an entity that has elected to be treated as a corporation for US tax purposes) must file Form 5472 if it is 25% or more foreign-owned and engages in reportable transactions with a foreign related party. "25% foreign-owned" means one or more foreign persons, directly or indirectly, own 25% or more of the stock by vote or value.

Category 2: Foreign-Owned Domestic Disregarded Entities (2017+)

Since December 13, 2017 (effective for tax years beginning after that date), Treas. Reg. §1.6038A-1(c) was expanded to treat certain foreign-owned domestic disregarded entities as corporations solely for purposes of the Form 5472 reporting requirements. A single-member LLC owned by a foreign person - which would normally be disregarded for all federal tax purposes - must file Form 5472 as if it were a corporation. The form is attached to a pro forma Form 1120 (a skeletal corporate return filed solely to carry the 5472).

This catches many foreign entrepreneurs completely off guard. A Russian, Israeli, or European entrepreneur forms a Delaware LLC to run their US e-commerce or consulting business. The LLC is owned 100% by the foreign individual. The LLC has no income tax obligations as a disregarded entity - it files a Schedule C on the owner's 1040NR or has no US filing at all if the owner is a nonresident. But the LLC must file Form 5472 with a pro forma 1120 by the 1120 due date (April 15 or March 15 for corporations, with extensions). Missing this filing: $25,000 penalty automatically.

Reportable Transactions: What Must Be Disclosed

A "reportable transaction" is any transaction between the reporting corporation and a foreign related party. The categories are broad:

Transaction TypeExamples
Sales of inventoryPurchases from or sales to a foreign parent or affiliate
Sales of property other than inventoryEquipment, intangibles, intellectual property transfers
Rents and royaltiesLicense fees paid to or received from foreign related parties
Loans and interestIntercompany loans, advances, interest payments
ServicesManagement fees, technical services, administrative services between related parties
CommissionsSales commissions paid to or received from foreign affiliates
Contributions and distributionsCapital contributions from the foreign owner, distributions to the foreign owner
Any other transactionCatch-all - any monetary or non-monetary transaction with a foreign related party

For foreign-owned SMLLCs treated as corporations solely for §6038A purposes, any transaction with the foreign owner - including the initial capitalization of the LLC and any distributions back to the owner - is a reportable transaction. Even a capital contribution of $1 from the foreign owner to the LLC triggers the filing requirement.

Who Is a "Foreign Related Party"?

A foreign related party for Form 5472 purposes is any foreign person who controls the reporting corporation or is controlled by the same persons who control the reporting corporation. Specifically:

  • A 25%-or-more direct or indirect foreign shareholder of the reporting corporation
  • Any foreign person of which the reporting corporation is a 25%-or-more shareholder
  • Any foreign person under common control with the reporting corporation (same 25% threshold)
  • Any officer or director of the reporting corporation who is a foreign person (for attribution purposes)

Filing Mechanics

For 25%-foreign-owned US corporations: Form 5472 is attached to the corporation's regular income tax return (Form 1120 for C-corps, Form 1120-S for S-corps, though S-corps cannot be 25% foreign-owned). The due date is the same as the corporation's return - March 15 for calendar-year corporations (April 15 for C-corps), with a 6-month automatic extension available.

For foreign-owned disregarded entities: Form 5472 is attached to a pro forma Form 1120. The pro forma 1120 only needs to include basic identifying information - name, address, EIN, tax year, and the attached 5472. No income, deductions, or tax is computed on the pro forma return. The filing is due April 15 for calendar-year entities (same as a C-corp) with a 6-month extension to October 15.

Obtaining an EIN

A foreign-owned SMLLC that has no other US tax filing obligations (no employees, no excise taxes, not elected to be treated as a corporation for income tax) must still obtain an Employer Identification Number (EIN) to file the pro forma 1120/5472. The EIN application (Form SS-4) can be filed by the foreign owner, but if the owner is a foreign person without a US SSN or ITIN, the EIN must be obtained by calling the IRS International EIN line directly - online application is not available without a US TIN.

Relationship to Form 5471

Form 5471 (Information Return of U.S. Persons With Respect to Certain Foreign Corporations) and Form 5472 are related but distinct. Form 5471 is filed by US persons who have interests in foreign corporations. Form 5472 is filed by US corporations with foreign owners reporting on transactions with those foreign owners. A US corporation with a foreign parent may need to file Form 5472, while the US person shareholders of a foreign parent may need to file Form 5471 - the same transaction can appear on both forms from different perspectives.

Penalty relief for first-time filers. The IRS has provided limited penalty relief for first-time failures to file Form 5472 by foreign-owned disregarded entities in certain circumstances through various notices. However, reliance on penalty relief requires demonstrating reasonable cause - ignorance of the law generally does not qualify. The safest approach is to identify the filing obligation before it arises - any time a foreign person forms or acquires a US LLC, the 5472 analysis should happen at formation, not after the penalty notice arrives.
