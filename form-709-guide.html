Form 709 Gift Tax Return: Annual Exclusion, Split Gifts & When to File

Form 709 (United States Gift and Generation-Skipping Transfer Tax Return) is required whenever a taxable gift is made - but "taxable gift" is a term of art that does not mean tax is owed. Gifts exceeding the annual exclusion are taxable gifts that must be reported and counted against the lifetime unified credit, even when no gift tax is actually paid. Many taxpayers - and some practitioners - incorrectly assume that because no tax is due, no return needs to be filed. The consequence of that error can affect the estate at death.

2026 Gift Tax Numbers

Annual exclusion: $19,000 per donee per year. Indexed for inflation in $1,000 increments.

Gift splitting (married couple): $38,000 per donee per year combined ($19,000 each).

Lifetime unified credit exemption: $15,000,000 per person (OBBBA permanent, indexed).

Gift tax rate: 40% on taxable gifts above the lifetime exemption.

Annual exclusion for gifts to non-citizen spouses: $190,000 (2026, indexed separately).

When Form 709 Must Be Filed

Form 709 must be filed for any year in which you make any of the following:

  • A gift of present-interest property to any one person exceeding $19,000 (2026)
  • A gift of a future-interest property of any amount (no annual exclusion available for future interests)
  • A gift splitting election with your spouse (Form 709 required even if the combined gift is under $38,000 if splitting is elected)
  • A gift to a skip person (two or more generations below you) subject to GST tax
  • A charitable deduction is claimed for a gift that is not entirely to charity
No tax due does not mean no filing required. A parent who gives a child $100,000 in appreciated stock will owe no gift tax if their lifetime exemption has not been exhausted. But they must still file Form 709 reporting the gift of $81,000 ($100K minus the $19K annual exclusion) as a taxable gift that reduces their remaining lifetime exemption. Not filing means the taxable gift is never reported against the exemption - creating an audit risk at death when the estate must reconstruct lifetime taxable gifts to determine the remaining applicable credit.

The Annual Exclusion: Present Interest Requirement

The $19,000 annual exclusion is only available for gifts of a present interest - the donee must have an immediate and unrestricted right to use, possess, or enjoy the property. Future interests (remainders, reversions, gifts in trust where the beneficiary cannot immediately access the funds) do not qualify for the annual exclusion.

The most common planning vehicle that converts future interests to present interests is the Crummey trust. By giving trust beneficiaries a temporary withdrawal right (typically 30 days after a contribution), the gift is treated as a present interest even though the trust holds the assets long-term. Proper documentation of Crummey notices to beneficiaries is essential to support the annual exclusion.

Gift Splitting: IRC §2513

Married couples can elect to split gifts, treating all gifts made by either spouse as made one-half by each. This effectively doubles the annual exclusion to $38,000 per donee per year and doubles the amount that can be transferred using the lifetime exemption. Gift splitting requires both spouses to consent by signing Form 709.

Gift splitting applies to all gifts made during the year - you cannot selectively split some gifts and not others. Once elected, the election is irrevocable for the entire year. If one spouse makes a gift to a relative of the other spouse, splitting is available as long as neither spouse has given anything to the other spouse's relatives as part of a plan to avoid gift tax.

Unlimited Exclusions: Tuition and Medical Payments

IRC §2503(e) provides unlimited exclusions for two specific types of payments - they are not subject to any dollar limit and do not require filing Form 709:

  • Direct tuition payments: Tuition paid directly to an educational organization for enrollment or attendance. This covers only tuition - not room and board, books, or other fees. Payment must go directly to the school, not to the student.
  • Direct medical payments: Amounts paid directly to a medical care provider for someone else's medical care. Payment must go directly to the provider - not reimbursement to the patient after they have paid.
A grandparent paying college tuition directly to the university can transfer unlimited value gift-tax-free on top of the $19,000 annual exclusion, on top of the lifetime exemption. A $60,000/year private university education costs $240,000 over four years of tuition alone. Paid directly to the school: $0 in taxable gifts, $0 Form 709 required, no reduction in the lifetime exemption. This is one of the most valuable and underused wealth transfer strategies available.

529 Superfunding: 5-Year Election

A donor can contribute up to 5 years' worth of annual exclusions to a 529 plan in one year - $95,000 per beneficiary ($190,000 for married couples gift splitting) - and elect to spread the contribution over 5 years for gift tax purposes. The election is made on Form 709. No additional annual exclusion gifts to that beneficiary can be made in years 2-5 without reducing the superfunding election. If the donor dies before the 5-year period ends, the unelapsed portion of the contribution is included in the estate.
