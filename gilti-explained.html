NCTI Explained (2026)

Net CFC Tested Income  •  Formerly Known as GILTI  •  IRC §951A  •  OBBBA (P.L. 119-21)
IRC §951A IRC §250 OBBBA P.L. 119-21
← cpavalidated.com

NCTI - Net CFC Tested Income - is the post-OBBBA renaming of GILTI (Global Intangible Low-Taxed Income). The One Big Beautiful Bill Act of 2025 (P.L. 119-21) eliminated the 10% QBAI exemption that previously sheltered a return on tangible assets, set the Section 250 deduction permanently at 40%, and reduced the foreign tax credit haircut from 20% to 10%. The result is a regime that is broader in scope (no QBAI shield), with a slightly higher effective rate (12.6% before FTC), more generous on foreign tax credits (90% creditability), and meaningfully different on the inclusion mechanics. This guide uses NCTI as the primary term and notes "GILTI" only to bridge readers familiar with the prior name.

The Rename in One Sentence

GILTI is now NCTI. Before OBBBA: "Global Intangible Low-Taxed Income," IRC §951A, 50% §250 deduction, 10.5% effective rate (corporate), 80% FTC. After OBBBA, for tax years beginning after December 31, 2025: "Net CFC Tested Income," same IRC §951A, 40% §250 deduction, 12.6% effective rate, 90% FTC, no QBAI shield. The substantive provisions are still in IRC §951A - the rebrand is statutory and reflects that the regime no longer pretends to target only intangible-driven income.

Who It Applies To

NCTI applies to any US shareholder - a US person who owns 10% or more of a Controlled Foreign Corporation (CFC). A CFC is a foreign corporation where US shareholders collectively own more than 50% of the stock by vote or value. IRC §957.

This includes US individuals, C-corporations, S-corporations, and partnerships that own CFC stock. The NCTI rules apply whether the owner is a large multinational or a US person who owns a small foreign company.

The NCTI Calculation (Post-OBBBA)

NCTI = Net CFC Tested Income (post-OBBBA, no QBAI subtraction)
Tested Income= CFC gross income minus IRC §954 exclusions (ECI, Subpart F, high-tax exclusion income, etc.)
-Tested Loss= Net loss of any CFC, used to offset tested income of other CFCs
= NCTI Inclusion Amount

The biggest mechanical change from the prior GILTI regime: no more QBAI exemption. Before OBBBA, US shareholders subtracted a 10% deemed return on the CFC's Qualified Business Asset Investment (Net DTIR) from tested income, on the theory that a normal return on tangible assets should not be swept into the regime. OBBBA eliminated that subtraction entirely. All tested income (after §954 exclusions) is now NCTI.

For asset-heavy CFCs - manufacturing, capital-intensive operations, certain real estate structures - this is the most adverse change in the international package. CFCs that historically generated little or no GILTI (because their tangible asset base produced enough QBAI return to absorb most income) will now produce sizeable NCTI inclusions.

Tax Rates on NCTI

Corporate (40% §250 ded.)
12.6%
21% corporate rate × (1 - 40% §250 deduction) = 12.6% effective. Permanent for tax years beginning after 12/31/2025. Pre-OBBBA: 10.5% (50% deduction).
Individual (no deduction)
Up to 37%
Individuals get no §250 deduction unless they make a §962 election. Without the election, NCTI is ordinary income at up to 37%.
Individual + §962 Election
~12.6%
§962 election allows the individual to be taxed as a corporation on NCTI, accessing the 40% §250 deduction. Second-level tax applies on actual distributions.

Foreign Tax Credit on NCTI - The 90% Rule

One of the more taxpayer-favorable changes under OBBBA: the foreign tax credit haircut on NCTI is reduced from 20% to 10%. C-corporations (and individuals making a §962 election) may now claim 90% of foreign taxes paid by their CFCs against NCTI liability. IRC §960(d).

If the CFC's foreign effective tax rate is approximately 14% or higher, the resulting US tax on NCTI should generally be fully offset by the FTC - assuming there is no expense allocation drag on the FTC limitation. OBBBA's allocation rules help here: interest expense and R&E expenses are no longer allocated against NCTI for purposes of the FTC limitation. Only directly allocable deductions (the 40% §250 deduction itself, and certain taxes) reduce foreign-source NCTI for limitation purposes. This is a significant improvement for highly leveraged or research-intensive multinationals.

The 14% breakeven (approximate). If the foreign tax rate on the CFC's tested income is at least roughly 14%, NCTI generally produces no incremental US tax for a C-corporation. Below 14%, residual US tax applies. The exact breakeven depends on FTC limitation mechanics and any expense allocation that survives the OBBBA changes. Above the breakeven, the high-tax exclusion may be the better election.

The Inclusion Shareholder Rule - New Under OBBBA

Pre-OBBBA, a US shareholder included GILTI and Subpart F income only if they held CFC stock on the last day of the CFC's tax year. OBBBA changes this: for tax years beginning after December 31, 2025, a US shareholder must include NCTI and Subpart F income if they held CFC stock at any time during the tax year - even if they exited before year end.

This is a structural change with real consequences:

  • M&A diligence must now consider mid-year inclusion exposure for both buyer and seller
  • Purchase agreements need indemnification language addressing pro-rata NCTI for the year of sale
  • Mid-year transfers between related parties no longer cleanly shift the inclusion to the year-end owner
  • Coordination is required between successive owners on items materially affecting the NCTI computation

Key Exclusions and Planning Tools

High-Tax Exclusion (HTE)

CFC income subject to an effective foreign tax rate of at least 90% of the US corporate rate may be excluded from tested income. For 2026 with a 21% US corporate rate, the threshold is 18.9%. The election is made annually and applies on a tested-unit basis under the regulations. Treas. Reg. §1.951A-2(c)(7). The HTE survived OBBBA unchanged.

Subpart F Income Exclusion

Income already included in the US shareholder's gross income under Subpart F (IRC §951) is excluded from tested income. No double taxation between Subpart F and NCTI. Subpart F generally captures passive, base-eroding, and certain foreign personal holding company income; NCTI then captures everything else above the (now zero) QBAI threshold.

IRC §962 Election (Individuals)

An individual US shareholder can elect to be treated as a corporation for purposes of NCTI. This unlocks the 40% §250 deduction and the deemed-paid foreign tax credit on NCTI inclusions, dropping the effective US rate from up to 37% to roughly 12.6%. The trade-off: actual cash distributions from the CFC are then subject to a second level of tax as if they were corporate dividends. The §962 election is annual. It is most valuable when the CFC's foreign effective tax rate is moderate and the individual faces high US marginal rates. IRC §962.

The 90% Foreign Tax Credit on NCTI

Foreign taxes paid by CFCs are creditable against NCTI liability, subject to limitations. The NCTI FTC basket is separate from the general FTC basket. OBBBA reduced the haircut from 20% to 10% (effective for tax years beginning after December 31, 2025) - so 90% of foreign taxes are now creditable, up from 80%. Foreign taxes in excess of the NCTI inclusion still cannot be carried forward in the NCTI basket. IRC §960(d).

Practical Example - The QBAI Effect

A US shareholder owns 100% of a foreign manufacturing CFC with $1,000,000 of net tested income and $5,000,000 of depreciable tangible property (QBAI = $5,000,000). The CFC pays 12% in foreign tax on its tested income.

Pre-OBBBA (GILTI through 2025)

Net DTIR = 10% × $5,000,000 = $500,000. GILTI = $1,000,000 - $500,000 = $500,000. For a C-corporation: 50% §250 deduction = $250,000 taxable, 21% × $250,000 = $52,500 US tax, less 80% × $120,000 foreign tax = $96,000 FTC. Result: no residual US tax, with FTC excess in the GILTI basket.

Post-OBBBA (NCTI for tax years beginning after 12/31/2025)

No QBAI subtraction. NCTI = $1,000,000. For a C-corporation: 40% §250 deduction = $600,000 taxable, 21% × $600,000 = $126,000 US tax. FTC: 90% × $120,000 = $108,000. Residual US tax = $126,000 - $108,000 = $18,000.

The QBAI elimination is the bite. Pre-OBBBA, this CFC produced no residual US tax. Post-OBBBA, the same fact pattern produces $18,000 of residual US tax on $1M of foreign income - even though the foreign tax rate (12%) is reasonably high. Capital-intensive CFCs that previously generated zero or trivial GILTI are now meaningfully exposed under NCTI.

Planning Considerations Under NCTI

Re-run the model. Any group with CFCs operating in jurisdictions with foreign tax rates between 8% and 18% should remodel under NCTI. The QBAI elimination + 40% §250 deduction + 90% FTC interact differently than the prior 10% QBAI + 50% §250 + 80% FTC.

HTE elections may matter more. With QBAI gone, the high-tax exclusion is the only mechanism (other than FTCs) to neutralize NCTI. Groups with mixed high- and low-tax CFCs should evaluate HTE annually on a tested-unit basis.

§962 election threshold has shifted. Individual US shareholders should re-run §962 modeling. The drop in §250 deduction (50% to 40%) makes the corporate-equivalent rate 12.6% instead of 10.5% - still attractive vs. 37% individual ordinary rates, but the breakeven point with cash-distribution second-level tax has moved.

Asset placement decisions are different now. Pre-OBBBA, locating tangible assets in CFCs generated QBAI return that sheltered income. That shield is gone. Combined with the FDDEI changes (FDII rebrand) that reward US-based IP serving foreign markets, the calculus for keeping IP and tangible production assets offshore has shifted.

M&A diligence on inclusion shareholder rule. Mid-year transactions involving CFCs need explicit purchase-agreement language addressing NCTI inclusions, FTC allocation, and information-sharing between buyer and seller. Standard "year-end ownership" assumptions in older indemnification clauses no longer track the statute.
