Hobby Loss Rules §183: When the IRS Says Your Business Is a Hobby

9-Factor Profit Motive Test • 3-of-5-Year Presumption • Income Taxable • Expenses Not Deductible • Documentation
IRC §183Treas. Reg. §1.183-2TCJA P.L. 115-97
← Individual Tax

If the IRS determines that an activity is a hobby rather than a business, the tax consequences are severe: under TCJA, hobby expenses are not deductible at all, but all hobby income remains fully taxable. Before TCJA, hobby expenses were deductible as miscellaneous itemized deductions subject to the 2% AGI floor - a bad result. Under TCJA and OBBBA, which permanently suspended miscellaneous itemized deductions, hobby expenses produce zero tax benefit. A person with $40,000 of hobby income and $35,000 of hobby expenses pays tax on the full $40,000 with no offset whatsoever. The hobby loss rules make the profit motive determination one of the highest-stakes classification questions in individual tax.

The Core Rule

Business: Activity engaged in for profit. All ordinary and necessary expenses deductible on Schedule C. Net loss deductible against other income (subject to at-risk and passive activity rules).

Hobby: Activity not engaged in for profit under §183. Income fully taxable. Expenses not deductible under TCJA/OBBBA permanent suspension of 2% floor miscellaneous itemized deductions. Net result: pay tax on gross hobby income.

The presumption: An activity is presumed to be a for-profit business if it shows a profit in at least 3 of the 5 consecutive tax years ending with the current year (2 of 7 for horse activities). This presumption is rebuttable by the IRS but shifts the burden.

The Nine-Factor Profit Motive Test

When the presumption does not apply, the IRS applies a nine-factor test from Treas. Reg. §1.183-2(b). No single factor is determinative; all facts and circumstances are weighed:

Factor 1 - Manner of operation: Does the taxpayer conduct the activity in a businesslike manner? Separate bank accounts, books and records, business plan, and professional practices all support profit intent.

Factor 2 - Expertise: Has the taxpayer consulted experts, studied the industry, or has prior experience in the activity? Seeking expert advice supports profit intent.

Factor 3 - Time and effort: Does the taxpayer devote substantial time to the activity? Hiring qualified employees to run the activity also counts.

Factor 4 - Asset appreciation: Does the taxpayer expect the assets used in the activity to appreciate in value? Overall profit including appreciation satisfies the profit requirement.

Factor 5 - Prior success: Has the taxpayer made a profit in similar activities in the past?

Factor 6 - History of income and losses: Continued losses beyond the startup period suggest hobby. But losses from startup difficulties, unforeseen circumstances, or depressed market conditions can be legitimate.

Factor 7 - Occasional profits: The size of profits relative to losses matters. Small occasional profits against large consistent losses suggest hobby.

Factor 8 - Financial status: Does the taxpayer have substantial other income that makes the tax benefits of losses attractive? Wealthy taxpayers claiming repeated losses from "businesses" receive heightened scrutiny.

Factor 9 - Elements of personal pleasure: Is the activity recreational or does it have personal appeal beyond profit? Horse breeding, photography, art, and travel-related activities face particular scrutiny.

The §183(e) election to postpone the profit determination: A taxpayer who begins a new activity may elect to postpone the IRS's ability to determine whether the activity is a hobby until after the fifth tax year (seventh for horses). This gives the taxpayer time to establish the profit track record needed for the 3-of-5 presumption. The election is made on Form 5213 (Election to Postpone Determination of Whether the Presumption Applies). It must be filed within 3 years of the due date of the return for the first tax year of the activity.

Documentation to Establish Profit Motive

The single most important defense in a hobby loss audit is contemporaneous documentation of profit intent. Maintain a separate business bank account and credit card. Keep a business plan - even a simple written document showing revenue projections, cost analysis, and growth strategy. Track time spent on the activity in a log. Document consultations with industry experts. Keep records of efforts to improve profitability. File Schedule C consistently. The IRS is less likely to characterize an activity as a hobby when the taxpayer can demonstrate that they approached it with the seriousness and discipline of someone trying to make money.
