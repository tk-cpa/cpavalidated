Home Sale Exclusion §121: $250K / $500K Capital Gain Tax-Free

$250K Single / $500K MFJ • 2-of-5-Year Test • Partial Exclusion Available • Home Office Recapture • Rental Conversion
IRC §121IRC §121(d)Treas. Reg. §1.121-1
← Real Estate

Section 121 provides one of the most valuable tax exclusions available to individual taxpayers: up to $250,000 of capital gain on the sale of a principal residence is excluded from gross income ($500,000 for married filing jointly). To qualify, the taxpayer must have owned and used the home as a principal residence for at least 2 of the 5 years preceding the sale. A taxpayer who sells their primary home for $700,000 with a $200,000 basis has a $500,000 gain - but if MFJ, the entire gain is excluded. Only the $0 above the exclusion amount is taxable. The exclusion can be used once every two years.

Ownership and Use Tests

Two separate tests, both required: The taxpayer must have (1) owned the home for at least 2 years during the 5-year period ending on the sale date, AND (2) used the home as a principal residence for at least 2 years during the same 5-year period. The 2 years of ownership and 2 years of use do not need to be the same 2 years, and they do not need to be continuous - periods of ownership and use can be aggregated.

MFJ $500,000 exclusion requirements: Either spouse must meet the ownership test; BOTH spouses must meet the use test; and neither spouse has excluded gain from a home sale in the prior 2 years.

Frequency limit: The §121 exclusion can be used once every 2 years. A taxpayer who used the exclusion in 2024 cannot use it again until 2026.

Partial Exclusion for Reduced Circumstances

A taxpayer who fails to meet the 2-year ownership and use tests may still claim a partial exclusion if the sale was due to a change in place of employment, health reasons, or unforeseen circumstances. The partial exclusion is proportional: (months of ownership and use / 24 months) times the full exclusion amount. A single taxpayer who lived in the home for 12 months and sells for a job relocation can exclude up to $125,000 (12/24 x $250,000) of gain. Unforeseen circumstances include divorce, death of a spouse, natural disasters, and qualified involuntary conversions - but not a general desire to move.

Home Office Recapture: Depreciation Cannot Be Excluded

Depreciation claimed on a home office cannot be excluded under §121. The portion of gain attributable to post-May 6, 1997 depreciation deductions is "unrecaptured §1250 gain" taxed at a maximum 25% rate - even if it falls within the §121 exclusion amount. A taxpayer who claimed $30,000 of home office depreciation over the years will recognize $30,000 of unrecaptured §1250 gain on the home sale regardless of the §121 exclusion. This is a permanent feature of the law and a common surprise for work-from-home taxpayers.

Claiming home office deductions reduces basis and creates a depreciation recapture obligation that survives the §121 exclusion. For many homeowners, the tax cost of depreciation recapture on home office deductions exceeds the annual deduction benefit. Model the total lifetime tax cost before claiming home office depreciation.

Rental-to-Residence Conversion

When a rental property is converted to a principal residence, the owner must use it as a primary residence for 2 of the 5 years before sale to claim §121. But the exclusion is limited: gain attributable to periods of "nonqualified use" (periods when the property was not the principal residence, after January 1, 2009) does not qualify for exclusion. The nonqualified use fraction is: (total nonqualified use days / total days owned). Only the remaining fraction of gain qualifies for exclusion. A property that was a rental for 3 years and then a primary residence for 2 years has 3/5 nonqualified use - 60% of the gain is excluded from §121 and 60% of the gain is taxable (in addition to depreciation recapture).
