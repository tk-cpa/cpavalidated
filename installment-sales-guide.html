Installment Sales: IRC §453, Gross Profit Percentage & §453A Interest

§453 Mechanics  •  Gross Profit Percentage  •  Recapture Front-Loading  •  §453A Interest on Large Deferrals  •  Related Party Rules
IRC §453 IRC §453A IRC §453(i)
← Business & Entity

An installment sale under IRC §453 allows a seller to recognize gain proportionally as payments are received rather than all in the year of sale. For business sales, real estate, and other appreciated assets, this can spread a large taxable gain over multiple years - reducing the immediate tax burden and potentially keeping the seller in lower brackets. The mechanics are straightforward in concept but contain several traps: depreciation recapture must be recognized in full in year one regardless of payments received, large deferrals trigger an interest charge under §453A, and related party installment sales face special acceleration rules.

When Installment Reporting Applies

Installment reporting is the default for qualifying sales - it applies automatically unless the taxpayer elects out. It applies to sales where at least one payment is received after the close of the tax year of sale. It does not apply to: dealer sales of property, sales of inventory, sales of publicly traded property (stocks, bonds), sales of depreciable property to a controlled entity (§453(g)), or recapture income under §1245/§1250 (recognized immediately regardless).

The Core Mechanics: Gross Profit Percentage

Installment Sale Computation
Step 1Gross Profit= Selling price minus adjusted basis minus selling expenses
Step 2Contract Price= Selling price minus liabilities assumed by buyer (but not less than gross profit)
Step 3Gross Profit Percentage= Gross Profit / Contract Price
Step 4Gain each year= Payment received x Gross Profit Percentage

Example: Business sold for $1,000,000. Adjusted basis $200,000. Selling expenses $50,000. Buyer assumes no debt. Gross Profit = $750,000. Contract Price = $1,000,000. Gross Profit Percentage = 75%. If the seller receives $250,000 in Year 1, gain recognized = $250,000 x 75% = $187,500. The remaining $562,500 of gain defers until future payments.

Depreciation Recapture: The Year-One Trap

IRC §453(i) requires that §1245 recapture income (accelerated depreciation on personal property) and §1250 recapture income (unrecaptured §1250 gain on real property at 25%) be recognized in full in the year of sale - regardless of how much cash is actually received. This is the most important trap in installment sale planning for business owners who have taken significant depreciation.

If the recapture exceeds the payment received in the year of sale, the seller can end up with a tax liability exceeding cash received. Model the recapture amount before structuring an installment deal to ensure liquidity is adequate to cover the first-year tax.

Cost segregation accelerates recapture. A building owner who used cost segregation to front-load depreciation into personal property categories may have substantial §1245 recapture that hits all in year one of an installment sale. The installment method elegantly defers capital gain but offers no relief on the recapture. Plan the sale structure before taking aggressive cost segregation if an installment sale exit is anticipated.

§453A: Interest Charge on Large Deferred Tax

IRC §453A imposes an interest charge when the total face amount of installment obligations arising from sales of certain property during the year exceeds $5,000,000. The interest charge applies to the deferred tax liability attributable to the installment obligations and is calculated at the applicable federal rate. This is not a penalty - it is an interest charge designed to capture the time value of the tax deferral when the amounts are large enough to be significant.

The §453A interest applies to: sales of property used in a trade or business or held for investment where the sales price exceeds $150,000, and only when total obligations exceed $5M. Below the $5M threshold, no §453A interest applies regardless of sale size.

Related Party Installment Sales: §453(e)

If a taxpayer sells property to a related party on the installment method and the related party then resells the property within two years, the original seller must recognize the remaining deferred gain immediately - in the year the second sale occurs. The purpose is to prevent basis-shifting through related parties: A sells to related party B on installment, B resells to an unrelated buyer at market value, and A defers gain while B has a stepped-up basis. §453(e) collapses this structure.

The two-year window is strictly enforced. If the related party buyer sells the property in month 23, the original seller recognizes the deferred gain. Planning the transaction timing to avoid the two-year rule is a common approach - but the IRS has challenged transactions where the timing appeared designed to avoid §453(e). Document legitimate business reasons for the timing of any resale.

The Opt-Out Election

A seller who does not want installment reporting can elect out by recognizing the full gain in the year of sale. This may be beneficial when: the seller has capital loss carryforwards to offset gain, tax rates are expected to increase significantly in future years, or the seller wants to simplify recordkeeping. The election is made on the return for the year of sale and is irrevocable.
