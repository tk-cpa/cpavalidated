International Information Returns: The Complete Compliance Map

Forms 5471, 5472, 8865, 8858, 3520, 3520-A, 8938, 8621  •  FinCEN 114 (FBAR)  •  Who Files, Penalties, Deadlines
IRC §6038 Series 31 USC §5314 Updated 2026
← cpavalidated.com

US persons with cross-border activity face a web of information reporting requirements that are separate from - and in addition to - income tax. These forms report interests in foreign corporations, partnerships, trusts, accounts, and assets. Failure to file triggers automatic penalties, often starting at $10,000 per form per year, and tolls the statute of limitations on the entire return. This guide maps every major international information return: who files it, what triggers it, and what the penalty is.

The Statute of Limitations Trap

Filing a required international information return late - or not at all - does not just trigger a standalone penalty. Under IRC §6501(c)(8), the statute of limitations on the entire tax return remains open indefinitely until the required form is filed and the IRS has had a reasonable time to process it. A missing Form 5471 can keep a return open for audit for years after the normal 3-year window closes.

Entity Reporting Forms

Form 5471 Information Return of US Persons With Respect to Certain Foreign Corporations
Who files
US persons who are officers, directors, or shareholders of certain foreign corporations. There are five filing categories (1-5) based on ownership percentage and type of transaction. Category 4 (10%+ US shareholders of CFCs) and Category 5 (10%+ shareholders subject to Subpart F/NCTI inclusions) are the most common.
Key triggers
Owning 10%+ of a foreign corporation's stock; becoming an officer or director of a foreign corporation in which a US person owns 10%+; acquisition or disposition of 10%+ interests; CFC status (US persons own 50%+ of voting power or value).
Penalty: $10,000 per form per year for failure to file. After 90 days of IRS notice, an additional $10,000 per 30-day period (up to $50,000 additional). IRC §6038(b).
Form 5472 Information Return of a 25% Foreign-Owned US Corporation or Foreign Corporation Engaged in US Trade or Business
Who files
US corporations that are 25% or more foreign-owned, and foreign corporations engaged in a US trade or business. The reporting party is the US corporation (or US agent of the foreign corporation), not the foreign shareholder. Also applies to disregarded entities (single-member LLCs) with a foreign owner.
What is reported
All reportable transactions between the reporting corporation and any related party: sales, services, rents, loans, licensing, dividends, contributions to capital, and distributions. Each transaction type reported separately.
Penalty: $25,000 per form per year (increased from $10,000 under TCJA). Each 30-day period after IRS notice adds another $25,000. IRC §6038A(d).
Form 8865 Return of US Persons With Respect to Certain Foreign Partnerships
Who files
US persons who control a foreign partnership (Category 1: 50%+ interest), own 10%+ while the partnership is controlled by US persons (Category 2), contribute property to a foreign partnership in exchange for an interest (Category 3), or acquire/dispose of a 10%+ interest (Category 4).
Key triggers
Forming a foreign partnership; contributing appreciated property to a foreign partnership; acquiring a 10%+ interest in a foreign partnership through purchase, gift, or inheritance. The form mirrors Form 1065 in structure.
Penalty: $10,000 per form per year (Category 1 and 2). Additional $10,000 per 30 days after IRS notice (up to $50,000). IRC §6038B penalties for Category 3. IRC §6038(b).
Form 8858 Information Return of US Persons With Respect to Foreign Disregarded Entities and Foreign Branches
Who files
US persons who own a foreign disregarded entity (a single-owner foreign entity that has not elected to be treated as a corporation); US persons who operate through a foreign branch. Also required when a US person owns a foreign DRE through a CFC or partnership.
What is reported
Balance sheet, income statement, and functional currency financial statements for the FDE or foreign branch. The form is often attached to Form 5471 when the FDE is owned by a CFC.
Penalty: $10,000 per form per year. Additional $10,000 per 30 days after IRS notice (up to $50,000). IRC §6038(b).

Trust and Gift Reporting

Form 3520 Annual Return To Report Transactions With Foreign Trusts and Receipt of Certain Foreign Gifts
Who files
US persons who: (a) transfer money or property to a foreign trust; (b) are treated as an owner of a foreign trust under the grantor trust rules (IRC §671-679); (c) receive distributions from a foreign trust; or (d) receive gifts or bequests from foreign persons exceeding $100,000 (individuals/estates) or $16,815 (2026, indexed) from foreign corporations or partnerships.
Key triggers
Inherited foreign assets through a foreign trust; received a large gift from a foreign relative; created a foreign trust for estate planning; are a beneficiary of a foreign family trust. The gift reporting threshold for foreign individuals is $100,000 per year aggregate.
Penalty: 35% of the gross value of distributions not reported; 35% of the gross value of property transferred to a foreign trust not reported; 5% of gross value of foreign trust assets per month (up to 25%) for failure to file as an owner. IRC §6677.
Form 3520-A Annual Information Return of Foreign Trust With a US Owner
Who files
The foreign trust itself (through a US agent) if it has a US grantor trust owner. Due March 15 (vs. April 15 for Form 3520). If the foreign trust does not file, the US owner must complete and attach a substitute Form 3520-A to their own return.
What is reported
Financial statements of the trust, income distributed or accumulated, US beneficiary information, and trust agent identification. This is the trust's own return - separate from the owner's Form 3520.
Penalty: $10,000 or 5% of gross value of trust assets, whichever is greater, for failure to file. IRC §6677(b).

Asset and Account Reporting

Form 8938 Statement of Specified Foreign Financial Assets (FATCA)
Who files
US taxpayers with specified foreign financial assets exceeding the applicable threshold. Filed with the income tax return (attached to Form 1040 or 1120). Thresholds: MFJ living in US: $100K year-end or $150K at any point; single/MFS living in US: $50K/$75K; living abroad thresholds are double. Does not replace FBAR.
What is reported
Foreign bank accounts, foreign stock and securities held outside a US financial institution, interests in foreign entities, foreign pension plans, foreign insurance with cash value, and other specified foreign financial assets. FBAR accounts are also reported here, but there is no overlap relief.
Penalty: $10,000 for failure to file; additional $10,000 per 30 days after IRS notice (up to $50,000). 40% accuracy penalty on understatements attributable to undisclosed SFFA. IRC §6038D.
FinCEN 114 (FBAR) Report of Foreign Bank and Financial Accounts
Who files
US persons (citizens, residents, entities) who have financial interest in or signature authority over foreign financial accounts where the aggregate maximum value exceeded $10,000 at any point during the calendar year. Filed separately from the tax return - electronically with FinCEN via BSA E-Filing System. Due April 15, auto-extension to October 15.
What counts as a foreign account
Foreign bank accounts, brokerage accounts, mutual fund accounts, foreign retirement plans with individual savings components, foreign insurance policies with cash value. Foreign accounts held through a US financial institution do not require FBAR reporting.
Penalty: Non-willful: up to $10,000 per form (per Bittner v. US, 2023). Willful: greater of $100,000 or 50% of account balance per year. Criminal penalties possible. 31 USC §5321.
Form 8621 Information Return by a Shareholder of a Passive Foreign Investment Company or QEF
Who files
US persons who are direct or indirect shareholders of a PFIC and who: receive excess distributions; recognize gain on disposition; make a QEF or MTM election; or are required to file under the annual filing requirement (direct shareholders with PFIC value over $25,000; indirect shareholders always).
Key triggers
Foreign mutual funds, ETFs, and many foreign holding companies qualify as PFICs. A US person who owns a foreign fund through a foreign brokerage almost certainly has PFIC reporting obligations. The annual PFIC statement from the fund is required for QEF elections.
Penalty: No standalone dollar penalty for failure to file Form 8621 - but failure to file tolls the statute of limitations on the entire return indefinitely. IRC §6501(c)(8).
Streamlined and voluntary disclosure programs. US persons who are non-willfully non-compliant with international information returns may qualify for the Streamlined Filing Compliance Procedures (domestic or foreign). Willful violations may require the IRS Voluntary Disclosure Program. Both programs allow taxpayers to come into compliance with reduced (or for foreign streamlined, zero) penalties.
