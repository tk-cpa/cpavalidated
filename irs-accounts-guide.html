IRS Online Accounts Guide

Individual Online Account  •  Business Tax Account  •  Tax Pro Account  •  ID.me Identity Verification
IRS.gov ID.me Updated 2026
← cpavalidated.com

The IRS operates three distinct online account systems, each with its own sign-in URL, registration path, and feature set. The Individual Online Account is for taxpayers managing their own SSN-based or ITIN-based filings. The Business Tax Account is for entities filing under an EIN. The Tax Pro Account is for licensed practitioners managing client authorizations. All three sit behind ID.me identity verification. This guide explains who needs which account, what each one does, and the direct authoritative IRS URLs to access them.

The Three Account Types in One Table

Individual Online Account. For taxpayers with a Social Security Number or ITIN. View balances, payments, transcripts, digital notices, and authorize tax pros. Required to view Forms W-2, 1099-DIV, 1099-INT, 1099-NEC, 1099-MISC, and 1095-A from your IRS file.

Business Tax Account. For sole proprietors with an EIN, partnerships, S-corporations, C-corporations, government entities, tax-exempt organizations, and Indian Tribal governments. View business balances, make federal tax deposits, view transcripts, and access K-1 information returns.

Tax Pro Account. For licensed practitioners. Submit and manage Power of Attorney (Form 2848) and Tax Information Authorization (Form 8821) with electronic signature, view client account balances, and access transcripts via the Transcript Delivery System.

Individual Online Account

The Individual Online Account is the core IRS taxpayer portal for anyone who files Form 1040, 1040-SR, or 1040-NR. It is the most feature-rich of the three account types and the one most taxpayers interact with directly.

Individual Online Account - Landing Page
irs.gov/payments/online-account-for-individuals
The official IRS information page describing the Individual Online Account features and registration process.
Individual Online Account - Direct Sign-In
sa.www4.irs.gov/ola
Direct authenticated sign-in URL. Requires an existing ID.me account or you will be prompted to create one.

What You Can Do

Per the IRS, an Individual Online Account allows the taxpayer to:

  • View balance owed by tax year and total amount due
  • View payment history and pending payments
  • Make and cancel payments, set up or modify payment plans
  • View key tax return information including AGI
  • Request and download tax transcripts (account, return, wage and income, record of account)
  • Receive 200+ digital IRS notices instead of paper
  • Approve and electronically sign Power of Attorney (Form 2848) and Tax Information Authorization (Form 8821) requests submitted by your tax pro
  • Get an Identity Protection PIN (IP PIN)
  • Download information returns: W-2, 1095-A, 1099-NEC, 1099-DIV, 1099-INT, 1099-MISC (capped at 100 1099-Bs); for tax years 2023, 2024, and 2025
The IP PIN is the single best fraud protection most taxpayers can enable. A six-digit Identity Protection PIN known only to you blocks anyone else from filing a return under your SSN. Once you opt in, the PIN is required on every return you file. The current-year PIN is generated annually around mid-December and visible only inside your Online Account. IRS Pub. 5367.

Common Issues

Foreign address. ID.me identity verification can be more difficult for U.S. persons living abroad. The IRS publishes specific guidance for taxpayers with foreign addresses who hold an SSN, and a separate path for ITIN holders.

ITIN holders. Identity verification through ID.me requires either an SSN or an ITIN. ID.me has a dedicated workflow for ITIN holders, but the document requirements differ.

Joint accounts. Each spouse needs their own Individual Online Account. There is no shared "married couple" account. When a return is filed jointly, each spouse can independently view that return's data through their respective accounts.

Address changes. Updating the email address on the IRS profile is done through the account itself. Changing the mailing address of record requires Form 8822 (or Form 8822-B for businesses) submitted by mail, or going through the address-change procedures on IRS.gov.

Business Tax Account

The Business Tax Account, originally launched in 2023 and steadily expanded, is the entity-side counterpart to the Individual Online Account. As of the most recent IRS rollouts, it is available to sole proprietors with an EIN, partnerships, S-corporations, C-corporations, government entities, tax-exempt organizations, and Indian Tribal governments. LLCs filing as sole proprietors on Schedule C or Schedule F are not yet supported under the EIN access path.

Roles and Access Levels

Business Tax Account uses a role-based access model:

What You Can Do

Per the IRS, a Business Tax Account allows authorized users to:

  • View business profile information and update contact details
  • View account balance by tax year and total amount owed
  • Make federal tax deposits (Form 941, 940, 944, 720, 990-T, 1041, 1042, 1120-series, 2290, 8804) and balance-due payments
  • View payment history
  • Access tax transcripts and tax compliance reports
  • View Schedule K-1 information returns (partners and shareholders)
  • Manage Designated User access for the entity
Renewal requirement for Designated Officials. Designated Official status must be renewed periodically. The IRS sends a notification during the 6-week renewal period; missing the window requires re-registration. Successor officers should plan for this transition before key employees leave.

Business Tax Account vs. EFTPS

Both Business Tax Account and EFTPS (Electronic Federal Tax Payment System) can be used to make federal tax deposits. They are not the same:

  • EFTPS is the underlying U.S. Treasury payment service. It is enrollment-based (PIN mailed by USPS), supports same-day or scheduled payments, requires a U.S. bank account, and is the only free way for businesses to schedule recurring federal tax deposits.
  • Business Tax Account is the IRS web portal that provides account visibility plus a payment interface. It can route payments through Direct Pay-like flows for balance-due transactions but is not a full replacement for EFTPS for ongoing payroll deposits.

Most active businesses use both: EFTPS for scheduled federal tax deposits, Business Tax Account for transcript access and balance visibility.

Tax Pro Account

The Tax Pro Account is the IRS portal for licensed practitioners (CPAs, EAs, attorneys, AFSP record-holders) to manage client representations electronically.

Tax Pro Account - Landing Page
irs.gov/tax-professionals/use-tax-pro-account
IRS information page describing Tax Pro Account features.
Tax Pro Account - Direct Sign-In
sa.www4.irs.gov/taxpro
Direct authenticated sign-in URL for the Tax Pro Account.

What You Can Do

Per the IRS, a Tax Pro Account allows licensed practitioners to:

  • Submit Power of Attorney (Form 2848) and Tax Information Authorization (Form 8821) requests electronically
  • Have the request signed electronically by the client through their Individual Online Account (eliminates the wet signature and CAF processing delay)
  • View account balance, tax records, and digital notices for authorized clients
  • Withdraw a POA / TIA
  • View clients' federal tax payment history

Tax Pro Account is distinct from e-Services, which is the broader practitioner suite that includes the Transcript Delivery System (TDS), e-file application management, and ACA Information Returns. Most CPAs and EAs use both; the boundary is roughly: Tax Pro Account for authorization workflow, e-Services for bulk transcript retrieval and information return transmission.

The CAF unit bottleneck is real. A wet-signed Form 2848 mailed or faxed to the CAF unit historically took 3-12 weeks to process during peak season, with returned-as-defective rates that varied. Electronic submission through Tax Pro Account, when the client approves through their Individual Online Account, generally posts in days rather than weeks. For time-sensitive collection or audit cases, this is the difference between meeting and missing a response deadline.

ID.me - The Identity Layer Behind All Three

The IRS does not maintain its own identity verification platform. All three account types sit behind ID.me, a private-sector identity verification service contracted by the federal government. A taxpayer creates one ID.me wallet and uses it for every IRS application that requires authenticated access.

IRS Identity Verification Overview
irs.gov/privacy-disclosure/how-to-register-for-irs-online-self-help-tools
Official IRS guidance on creating and verifying an ID.me account, including alternatives for taxpayers who cannot complete the standard verification.

What ID.me Verifies

The ID.me wallet uses a combination of:

  • Government-issued photo ID (driver license, state ID, passport)
  • Selfie or video selfie (matched to the ID photo)
  • Social Security Number or ITIN
  • Phone number tied to the taxpayer's name (for trusted device check)
  • Multi-factor authentication (authenticator app, SMS code, or hardware key)

Common Verification Errors

Error CodeMeaningFix

EC 6101Verified identity without an SSN or ITINRe-verify with an SSN or ITIN; the IRS does not allow access via document-only verification

If the standard self-service verification cannot be completed, ID.me offers a video call ("Trusted Referee") path to verify in person. There is also a paper alternative for taxpayers who cannot complete digital verification - though it is significantly slower and primarily limits the user to certain transcript-by-mail and notice-by-mail options.

Account credentials are personal. The IRS specifically prohibits sharing of ID.me credentials. A tax professional cannot log in as the client. The correct workflow is the Tax Pro Account submitting an authorization request that the client electronically approves through their own Individual Online Account.

Other IRS Sign-In Systems

Beyond the three main accounts, the IRS operates several specialized portals:

e-Services
irs.gov/e-services
Practitioner suite. Includes e-file application management, Transcript Delivery System (TDS) for bulk transcripts, ACA Information Returns transmission, and IRIS application for transmitter control codes.
IRIS - Information Returns Intake System
irs.gov/filing/e-file-providers/information-returns-intake-system-iris
Free IRS portal for filing information returns (1099-series). Replaces FIRE for most filers. Supports Form 1099-NEC, 1099-MISC, 1099-INT, 1099-DIV, 1099-K, 1099-R, and others.
EFTPS - Electronic Federal Tax Payment System
eftps.gov
U.S. Treasury payment system. Enrollment-based, PIN mailed by USPS. Free service for scheduled and same-day federal tax payments. Required for most business federal tax deposits.
Login.gov
login.gov
An alternative federal identity service. Currently used by the IRS for limited applications only - the primary path remains ID.me. Login.gov does not currently provide access to the Individual Online Account or Business Tax Account.

Practical Recommendations

For individuals: Create the Individual Online Account before tax season starts. Verifying ID.me identity in February or March, when call volumes spike, can take days. Done in advance, the account is ready when needed.

For small businesses: Whoever is the practical record-keeper (founder, CFO, controller) should hold Designated Official status, and at least one backup person should be designated. Business Tax Account loss-of-access during a transition is a recurring pain point, particularly when the prior Designated Official has left the company without renewing the role.

For practitioners: Use Tax Pro Account for routine POA/TIA submissions on individual clients. The CAF unit's wet-signature backlog makes electronic authorization the operationally correct default. Reserve Form 2848 mail/fax submission for cases where the client cannot authenticate to their own Individual Online Account.

For all taxpayers: Enable the IP PIN. It is the single highest-leverage protection against return-related identity theft, and the only material downside is having to remember to use it on next year's filing.
