IRS Payments Guide

Direct Pay  •  EFTPS  •  Same-Day Wire  •  Check / Form 1040-V  •  Card  •  Foreign
IRS.gov EFTPS.gov Updated 2026
← cpavalidated.com

There is no single "best" way to pay the IRS. The right method depends on whether the payment is individual or business, whether it must be received the same day, whether the payer is in the U.S. or abroad, and how much friction the payer is willing to accept. This guide covers every IRS-supported federal tax payment method, with the verified authoritative URL and direct link to the relevant IRS form or worksheet for each. The wire instructions in this guide are taken directly from the IRS Same-Day Taxpayer Worksheet PDF; the check instructions follow Form 1040-V (2025).

The Five Practical Choices

1. Direct Pay (free, individual taxpayers). Best for one-off Form 1040 balance-due or estimated tax payments. No enrollment.

2. EFTPS (free, individuals and businesses). Best for recurring or scheduled payments, especially business payroll deposits. Requires advance enrollment.

3. Same-day wire (bank fee applies). Best for last-minute payments where Direct Pay or EFTPS will not post in time. Cutoff is 5 p.m. ET.

4. Check or money order with Form 1040-V (free, slow). Best when electronic payment is impractical. Add 5-10 business days for posting.

5. Debit or credit card (processor fee applies). Best when float, rewards, or short-term financing is the priority and the fee is acceptable.

1. IRS Direct Pay

Direct Pay is the IRS's free direct-debit service for individual taxpayers. No enrollment is required - the user provides identity verification information and bank account details for each transaction. Best suited for one-off Form 1040 balance-due, estimated tax, extension, and amended return payments.

IRS Direct Pay
irs.gov/payments/direct-pay-with-bank-account
Pay directly from a U.S. checking or savings account. Free, no enrollment, supports payments scheduled up to 365 days in advance, modifications and cancellations up to 2 business days before the scheduled date.

Limits: Up to two Direct Pay transactions per 24-hour period. Maximum $10 million per transaction. Individual taxpayers only - businesses use EFTPS.

Confirmation: Direct Pay generates an immediate confirmation number. Save it. The IRS will not be able to research a "missing" payment without it.

2. EFTPS - Electronic Federal Tax Payment System

EFTPS is the U.S. Treasury's master payment service for federal tax. It supports both individuals and businesses, requires enrollment (PIN mailed by USPS, typically 5-7 business days), and is the standard tool for ongoing business federal tax deposits and scheduled estimated tax payments.

EFTPS Sign-In and Enrollment
eftps.gov
Direct access to EFTPS online and enrollment. Free service from the U.S. Department of the Treasury. Supports both individual and business payments.
IRS EFTPS Information Page
irs.gov/payments/eftps-the-electronic-federal-tax-payment-system
IRS overview of EFTPS, including enrollment, payment scheduling, and customer service phone numbers.

EFTPS Enrollment

  1. Visit eftps.gov and select Enrollment. Provide EIN (business) or SSN (individual), bank account and routing number, and name/address as on the IRS file.
  2. Wait 5-7 business days for the PIN by mail.
  3. Call the EFTPS enrollment line to receive an Internet password.
  4. Sign in at eftps.gov to schedule payments. Payments must be initiated by 8 p.m. ET the day before the due date for next-day posting.
EFTPS for U.S. persons abroad. EFTPS requires a U.S. bank account. Foreign bank accounts cannot be used. U.S. persons abroad with no U.S. bank account should use the foreign wire path or pay by check from a U.S.-payable instrument.

3. Same-Day Wire

When a payment must be received the same day - most often because Direct Pay or EFTPS will not post in time - a same-day wire through the taxpayer's financial institution is the option. The taxpayer completes the IRS Same-Day Taxpayer Worksheet, brings it to their bank, and the bank initiates the wire to the Federal Tax Collection Service.

IRS Same-Day Wire Information Page
irs.gov/payments/same-day-wire-federal-tax-payments
Official IRS page describing the same-day wire process. The page directs taxpayers to download the Same-Day Taxpayer Worksheet PDF and present it to their financial institution.
Same-Day Taxpayer Worksheet (PDF)
download.eftps.gov/SameDayPaymentWorksheet.pdf
The official worksheet to bring to your financial institution. Includes Common IRS Tax Type table (Form numbers and suffixes for 720, 940, 941, 944, 945, 990-T, 1040, 1042, 1120, 2290, 8804, 8963).

Confirmation

After the wire is sent, the EFT acknowledgment number can be obtained by calling the Federal Tax Collection Service at 1-800-382-0045 and following the automated prompts. EFTPS-enrolled taxpayers can also retrieve the EFT number on the next business day at eftps.gov or by calling 1-800-605-9876.

The wire trap. Wires received after 5 p.m. ET are rejected and returned to the financial institution. Errors in formatting (wrong tax type code, wrong period, missing TIN) also cause rejection. A rejected wire is not a paid tax. If the deadline lands on a wire-rejection day, a late-payment penalty plus interest apply. Initiate the wire with at least 2-3 hours of cushion, and confirm receipt of the EFT number before assuming the payment is complete.

4. Foreign Wire (International Payments)

When the taxpayer has no U.S. bank account, they cannot use Direct Pay or EFTPS. The IRS supports international wire payments through the same-day wire infrastructure, but routed through the taxpayer's foreign bank with the receiving institution still being the Federal Tax Collection Service.

IRS Foreign Electronic Payments - Tax Type Codes
irs.gov/individuals/international-taxpayers/foreign-electronic-payments-tax-type-codes
Official IRS guidance for international wire transfers. Use the same Same-Day Taxpayer Worksheet, with the proper Tax Type Code and tax period, and present it to the foreign bank for international wiring.

Per the IRS: "U.S. tax must be remitted to the U.S. Internal Revenue Service in U.S. dollars." The foreign bank handles currency conversion. Foreign bank fees are typically much higher than domestic wire fees - the IRS notes that other options including credit card may be more cost-effective for smaller payments.

Currency conversion timing. The wire amount in U.S. dollars must equal the U.S. tax due. If the foreign bank converts at a less favorable rate than expected, the U.S. dollar amount received may be short. Either build in a small buffer or specify the U.S. dollar amount and let the foreign bank pull the equivalent in local currency.

5. Pay by Check or Money Order

The traditional method. The IRS still supports it, but it is the slowest and most error-prone. Form 1040-V (Payment Voucher) is the slip used to ensure the payment is credited to the correct taxpayer and tax year.

IRS Pay by Check or Money Order
irs.gov/payments/pay-by-check-or-money-order
Official IRS instructions for paying by check. Includes the mailing address table by state.
Form 1040-V Payment Voucher (PDF)
irs.gov/pub/irs-pdf/f1040v.pdf
The current Form 1040-V. Page 2 lists the IRS mailing address by state. Use the address that matches the state on the return, not the state where the taxpayer currently lives if different.

How to Write the Check Correctly (Form 1040-V Instructions)

  1. Make the check or money order payable to "U.S. Treasury". Do not abbreviate. Do not write "IRS" - the IRS form instructions specifically reject that.
  2. Enter the amount on the right side of the check exactly as $XXX.XX. No dashes, no lines, no "and 00/100" notation.
  3. On the memo line: SSN (or first SSN if joint return), tax year, and the form number ("2025 Form 1040" or "2025 Form 1040-NR" as applicable).
  4. Include the daytime phone number on the check.
  5. Do not staple or paper-clip the check to Form 1040-V or to the return. The IRS specifically asks that they be enclosed loose.
  6. Use regular USPS mail. The IRS specifically advises that "private delivery services may delay your payment processing" for Form 1040-V mailings (the Pay-by-Check page note).

Common Mistakes

  • Writing "IRS" instead of "U.S. Treasury" - the form instructions reject this.
  • Using the wrong lockbox address - addresses are by state and change periodically. Always verify the current address on Form 1040-V page 2 before mailing.
  • Stapling the check to the voucher - slows processing.
  • Mailing to the return-filing address instead of the payment address. Returns and payments often go to different lockboxes.
  • Underdating the check (post-dating). Treasury will process when received; post-dated checks may be rejected by the bank.
The "MFT 31 separate assessment" memo line. If the taxpayer filed a joint return but received a separate assessment notice (typical in innocent spouse, injured spouse, or post-divorce collection cases), the IRS instructs the payer to include "MFT 31 separate assessment" on the memo line so the credit goes to the correct individual rather than the joint balance.

6. Pay by Debit or Credit Card

The IRS does not directly accept card payments - it uses third-party processors. There are typically two or three approved processors at any given time, each with their own fee schedule.

IRS Pay by Debit or Credit Card
irs.gov/payments/pay-your-taxes-by-debit-or-credit-card
Lists current approved processors and their fees. Verify the current fee schedule before paying - rates and processors change.

Debit card fees are flat dollar amounts (typically $2-3 per transaction). Credit card fees are percentages (typically 1.75% to 1.85% of the payment amount). For very small balances, a flat-fee debit transaction may be cheaper than the percentage. For very large balances, percentage fees can be substantial - a $50,000 tax payment at 1.85% costs $925 in processor fees, before any credit card interest.

Card payments do not receive same-day IRS posting. Allow 1-2 business days for the IRS to receive the payment from the processor.

7. Electronic Funds Withdrawal (Pay-with-Return)

When e-filing through tax software or a tax preparer, the taxpayer can authorize a one-time electronic funds withdrawal at the time of filing. The taxpayer specifies the U.S. bank account, routing number, and the date the IRS should pull the funds (any date from the filing date to the original due date).

This is functionally a delayed Direct Pay - free, with no enrollment, but only available at e-file time. The withdrawal can be cancelled by calling the IRS at least 2 business days before the scheduled date.

8. Pay by Cash (Retail Partners)

Cash payment is supported through retail partners (currently selected 7-Eleven, Walgreens, CVS, and others depending on rotation). Maximum $1,000 per day per transaction. The taxpayer must register online first at the official payment provider's site, receive a barcode, and present the barcode and cash at the retail partner.

The IRS specifically states: do not send cash through the mail. Currency mailed to the IRS is not insured and can be lost.

9. Online Payment Agreement (Installment Plans)

If the taxpayer cannot pay the full balance, an installment agreement is available through the Online Payment Agreement application. Setup fees vary by plan type:

  • Short-term (up to 180 days): No setup fee. Full balance must be paid within the period.
  • Long-term direct debit: $22-$31 setup fee (online application).
  • Long-term non-direct-debit: $69-$130 setup fee.
  • Low-income taxpayers (at or below 250% of federal poverty level) qualify for reduced or waived setup fees.
IRS Online Payment Agreement Application
irs.gov/payments/online-payment-agreement-application
Apply for short-term or long-term installment plans online. Failure-to-pay penalties continue to accrue (at half the normal rate) during a valid installment agreement, but failure-to-file penalties are stopped.

Interest on the unpaid balance continues to accrue regardless of the installment agreement. As of Q2 2026, the underpayment interest rate is 7%; check the current rate before relying on this number, as the IRS adjusts quarterly under IRC §6621.

Penalties for Late Payment

Two separate penalties apply when tax is paid late:

When both failure-to-file and failure-to-pay apply concurrently, the failure-to-pay reduces the failure-to-file penalty so the combined rate is 5% per month, not 5.5%. The failure-to-pay continues until the balance is paid.

An extension of time to file is not an extension of time to pay. Form 4868 (individual) and Form 7004 (entity) extend the filing deadline. They do not extend the payment deadline. Tax owed must still be paid by the original due date - typically April 15 for individuals - to avoid failure-to-pay penalty and underpayment interest. The extension only avoids the much larger failure-to-file penalty.

Practical Recommendations

For most individual taxpayers: Use Direct Pay for one-off Form 1040 balance-due payments. Schedule estimated tax through Direct Pay or EFTPS. Avoid checks unless there is a specific reason - they post slowly and the misposting risk is real.

For active businesses: EFTPS for federal tax deposits and scheduled payments. Business Tax Account for visibility and one-off balance-due payments. Keep a small operational reserve for same-day wire capability when a deadline is missed.

For taxpayers abroad: If a U.S. bank account exists, use Direct Pay (individual) or EFTPS (business). If only foreign banking is available, foreign wire is the only practical electronic option - and credit card may be cheaper for smaller payments. Mailed checks from abroad can take 2-4 weeks; not recommended near deadlines.

For practitioners: Steer clients toward electronic payment in every case where it is feasible. Misapplied checks generate notices that consume hours of professional time per occurrence. The aggregate fee impact of a small number of avoidable check-related notices typically exceeds the marginal cost of helping clients enroll in EFTPS or use Direct Pay.
