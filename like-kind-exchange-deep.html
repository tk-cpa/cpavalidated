1031 Exchange Deep Dive: Boot, Identification Rules & Reverse Exchanges

Boot Calculation  •  45/180-Day Rules  •  3-Property / 200% / 95% Tests  •  Reverse Exchanges  •  Related Party Rules
IRC §1031 Treas. Reg. §1.1031 Rev. Proc. 2000-37
← cpavalidated.com

A properly structured IRC §1031 exchange defers all gain recognition on the sale of investment or business property - potentially indefinitely, or until death when the step-up in basis eliminates the deferred gain entirely. But the rules are exacting: two hard deadlines, strict identification requirements, a qualified intermediary requirement, and a series of traps involving boot, related parties, and property that was used personally. One wrong step disqualifies the exchange.

Boot: What Triggers Gain Recognition

The exchange is fully tax-deferred only if no "boot" is received. Boot is anything received in the exchange that is not like-kind property - cash, net debt relief, non-like-kind property, or personal property. Gain is recognized to the extent of boot received, but never more than the total realized gain.

Boot Calculation
Boot= Cash received
+FMV of non-like-kind property received
+Net debt reliefDebt on relinquished property assumed by buyer MINUS debt on replacement property assumed by taxpayer; if negative, no boot from debt
=Total boot received
Gain recognized= Lesser of total boot OR total realized gain
Trading down in value always triggers boot. If the relinquished property sold for $2M with $500K of debt, and the replacement property costs $1.8M with $400K of debt, the taxpayer received $100K of net debt relief ($500K - $400K) and $200K less in replacement property value. Total boot = $300K. Gain is recognized to the extent of $300K (or total gain, if less). To defer all gain, the taxpayer must replace both the equity AND the debt - either by acquiring replacement property of equal or greater value and taking on equal or greater debt, or by adding cash to make up the difference.

The Two Hard Deadlines

Both deadlines run from the date of transfer of the relinquished property and are absolute - there are no extensions for weekends, holidays, or circumstances outside the taxpayer's control (except for federally declared disasters per Rev. Proc. 2018-58).

DeadlineDaysWhat Must Be Done
Identification Period45 calendar daysIdentify replacement property in writing to the qualified intermediary. The identification must be unambiguous - legal description or street address. Oral identifications do not count.
Exchange Period180 calendar days (or tax return due date including extensions, whichever is earlier)Close on the replacement property. Note: if the 180th day falls after the tax return due date (April 15), file an extension to get the full 180 days.
The tax return due date trap on the 180-day rule. For a taxpayer who transfers the relinquished property in October, the 180-day exchange period extends into April of the following year. If the taxpayer files the return by April 15 without an extension, the exchange period ends on April 15 - not day 180. Always file an extension (Form 4868) when a 1031 exchange straddles year-end to preserve the full 180-day window.

The Three Identification Rules

The taxpayer must identify replacement property within 45 days using one of three alternative tests. Only one test applies per exchange - properties identified must satisfy the chosen test.

RuleHow It WorksBest For
3-Property RuleIdentify up to 3 properties regardless of fair market value. Most commonly used.Most exchanges - simple and flexible; allows backup properties if first choice falls through
200% RuleIdentify any number of properties as long as their aggregate FMV does not exceed 200% of the FMV of the relinquished property.Large portfolios where more than 3 properties are desired but the math works
95% RuleIdentify any number of properties regardless of value, but must acquire 95% or more of the aggregate FMV of all identified properties.Rarely used in practice because it requires near-total acquisition of identified properties
Identify more than you need. Under the 3-property rule, identify all three properties even if the first choice is highly probable. If the deal on the first property falls through on day 44, the taxpayer cannot add a new replacement property after day 45. Having two backup properties identified gives optionality without risk.

The Qualified Intermediary Requirement

The taxpayer cannot receive the sale proceeds directly - doing so triggers immediate gain recognition on the entire amount. A qualified intermediary (QI) must hold the proceeds from the relinquished property sale and use them to acquire the replacement property on the taxpayer's behalf. The QI cannot be the taxpayer, a related party, or anyone who has been the taxpayer's agent (attorney, accountant, real estate broker, employee) within the prior two years.

The QI's financial stability matters - if the QI becomes insolvent or misappropriates funds, the taxpayer's exchange may fail and the funds may be unrecoverable. Use a well-capitalized, financially sound QI with appropriate insurance and bonding. Large national title companies typically have QI divisions.

Reverse Exchanges: Rev. Proc. 2000-37

A reverse exchange allows the taxpayer to acquire the replacement property before selling the relinquished property - the opposite of a standard deferred exchange. This is useful when the replacement property is available but the relinquished property has not yet sold. However, the taxpayer cannot own both properties simultaneously - the IRS requires an Exchange Accommodation Titleholder (EAT) to hold one of the properties while the exchange is completed.

Rev. Proc. 2000-37 provides a safe harbor for reverse exchanges: the EAT must hold one property, the taxpayer must identify the relinquished property within 45 days of the EAT acquiring the replacement property, and the exchange must be completed within 180 days. Reverse exchanges are more expensive than forward exchanges (EAT fees, additional legal structure) but provide flexibility when timing constraints favor acquiring the replacement property first.

Related Party Rules: IRC §1031(f)

Exchanges with related parties face additional restrictions designed to prevent basis-shifting transactions. If the taxpayer exchanges with a related party, both the taxpayer and the related party must hold the exchanged properties for at least 2 years after the exchange. If either party disposes of their property within 2 years, the deferred gain from the exchange becomes immediately taxable.

Related parties for §1031(f) purposes include: family members (siblings, spouse, ancestors, lineal descendants), and controlled entities (corporations or partnerships in which the taxpayer owns more than 50%). The 2-year holding requirement applies regardless of whether the subsequent disposition is itself another §1031 exchange.

Basis in Replacement Property

Basis in Replacement Property
=FMV of replacement property
-Deferred gainGain not recognized in the exchange
+Gain recognized (boot)Adds back gain that was taxed
=Substituted basisEquals the adjusted basis of relinquished property + any gain recognized + cash paid - boot received

The low substituted basis in the replacement property preserves the deferred gain for future recognition - either on a future taxable sale or at death, when the step-up in basis under IRC §1014 eliminates the deferred gain entirely. This is the "die and defer" strategy: exchange repeatedly to defer gain, hold until death, and pass the property with stepped-up basis to heirs who can sell immediately with no tax on the appreciation that accumulated during the owner's lifetime.
