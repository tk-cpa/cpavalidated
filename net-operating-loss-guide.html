Net Operating Loss (NOL): §172, 80% Limit & OBBBA Rules

Indefinite Carryforward  •  80% Taxable Income Cap  •  Farming 2-Year Carryback  •  §461(l) Excess Business Loss  •  Updated 2026
IRC §172 IRC §461(l) Updated 2026
← cpavalidated.com

A net operating loss arises when a taxpayer's deductions exceed gross income for the year. The NOL can be carried to other tax years to reduce tax liability - but TCJA changed the rules significantly, and OBBBA made those changes permanent. Carrybacks are eliminated for most taxpayers. Carryforwards are indefinite but limited to 80% of taxable income in each carry year. Understanding how to compute, track, and utilize an NOL is essential for businesses with cyclical income, early-stage companies, and individuals with large deductible losses.

Current NOL Rules - Post-TCJA, Post-OBBBA

NOLs arising after 2017: Indefinite carryforward only. No carryback (with exceptions). Limited to 80% of taxable income in the carryforward year.

NOLs arising before 2018: Old rules apply - 2-year carryback, 20-year carryforward, 100% offset (no 80% limitation).

Farming NOLs: 2-year carryback election available regardless of the general carryback prohibition.

Property/casualty insurance company NOLs: 2-year carryback, 20-year carryforward.

OBBBA: Made TCJA's carryback prohibition and 80% limitation permanent - no sunset.

Computing the NOL: What Goes In

An individual's NOL is not simply the negative number on the bottom of their Form 1040. Certain deductions must be excluded from the NOL computation because they are "non-business" items that should not create a loss that offsets business income in other years.

For corporations, the NOL computation is simpler - it is generally the negative taxable income for the year, without the non-business adjustments required for individuals. C-corporations cannot deduct dividends received deduction when computing the NOL.

The 80% Limitation: How It Works in Practice

NOLs arising after December 31, 2017 can only offset 80% of taxable income in the carryforward year. The remaining 20% is always subject to tax - even if the taxpayer has a large NOL carryforward. The 80% is computed before the NOL deduction itself.

The 80% limit means an NOL carryforward never fully offsets income - it extends the NOL utilization. Example: $1,000,000 NOL carryforward. In year 2, taxable income (before NOL) = $500,000. Maximum NOL deduction = 80% x $500,000 = $400,000. Taxable income after NOL = $100,000. Remaining NOL carryforward = $600,000. The NOL is not consumed faster than the taxable income - it stretches across more years than would have been the case under the old unlimited offset rules.

No Carryback for Most NOLs - Exceptions

TCJA eliminated the general 2-year carryback for NOLs arising after 2017. OBBBA made this permanent. However, several exceptions remain:

Farming NOL carryback election. Farmers can elect to carry back an NOL 2 years, which can produce an immediate refund of prior-year taxes paid. The election is made on the return for the NOL year. Because farming income is highly variable - drought, commodity price swings, disaster losses - the carryback is particularly valuable. If no carryback election is made, the farming NOL carries forward indefinitely subject to the 80% limitation.

The Excess Business Loss Limitation: §461(l)

Before an NOL can even be created at the individual level, another limitation applies: the excess business loss (EBL) limitation under IRC §461(l). For non-corporate taxpayers (individuals, partners, S-corp shareholders), aggregate business losses cannot exceed $313,000 (single) / $626,000 (MFJ) in 2026, adjusted for inflation. Losses in excess of these thresholds are not allowed in the current year - they are treated as an NOL carryforward to the following year.

The EBL limitation applies after the passive activity and at-risk rules. It was originally a TCJA provision, extended by CARES Act, and made permanent by OBBBA. The effect: large business losses from pass-through entities are capped at the threshold in the loss year, with the excess automatically becoming an NOL for the following year.

§461(l) and §172 interact in sequence. A large Schedule C or K-1 loss first passes through basis, at-risk, and passive activity limitations. Amounts that survive those limitations are subject to §461(l). Any excess over the EBL threshold becomes an NOL carryforward subject to the 80% limitation. Practitioners must track all three layers simultaneously for clients with large pass-through losses.

Tracking and Documenting NOL Carryforwards

NOL carryforwards must be tracked carefully across years, especially when they span different tax regimes (pre-2018 vs. post-2017). The IRS expects taxpayers to maintain records of: the year the NOL arose, the amount of the NOL, any amounts used in prior carryforward years, and the remaining carryforward. Form 1045 (Application for Tentative Refund) can be used to carry back eligible NOLs for a quick refund. The NOL is reported on Schedule A of Form 1045 or on the return itself using Form 1040 Schedule 1.

If an NOL is carried forward into a year when the taxpayer's return is audited and the NOL year is outside the statute of limitations, the IRS can still examine the NOL computation to verify its amount - the NOL year does not need to be open for audit for the IRS to contest the carryforward amount.
