PFIC Deep Dive: Excess Distribution, QEF & Mark-to-Market

A passive foreign investment company - PFIC - is the most punitive tax classification in the US international tax code. The default tax regime imposes ordinary income rates plus an interest charge on gains and distributions, regardless of how long you held the investment. Foreign mutual funds, ETFs, and many foreign holding companies are PFICs. Most US persons who own them don't know it until the damage is done.

What Is a PFIC - IRC §1297(a)

A foreign corporation is a PFIC if it meets either of two tests for any taxable year: (1) Income test: 75% or more of its gross income is passive income. (2) Asset test: 50% or more of its assets produce or are held to produce passive income (measured by average fair market value, or adjusted basis for non-publicly traded companies).

Passive income includes dividends, interest, rents, royalties, annuities, and net gains from disposition of property. A foreign mutual fund investing in stocks and bonds almost certainly meets both tests. So does a foreign holding company that holds investment assets rather than operating businesses.

The Three Tax Regimes

Once a company is classified as a PFIC, the US shareholder must choose how to be taxed. There are three regimes - and the default (§1291) is the worst.

The §1291 Excess Distribution Regime: How Bad Is It?

The §1291 regime is designed to eliminate any tax deferral benefit from holding a PFIC. It works by treating every "excess distribution" and all gain on disposition as if it had been earned ratably over the entire holding period, then charging ordinary tax rates plus interest on each prior year's allocation.

An excess distribution is any distribution that exceeds 125% of the average distributions received in the prior 3 years (or a shorter period if held less than 3 years). If a PFIC distributes $10,000 in a year when the 3-year average was $5,000, the excess is $3,750 ($10,000 - 125% x $5,000). The first $6,250 (the non-excess portion) is taxed normally; the excess $3,750 goes through the §1291 allocation.

QEF Election: The Right Answer When Available

The Qualified Electing Fund election under IRC §1293 is the preferred treatment. The shareholder elects to include their pro-rata share of the PFIC's ordinary earnings and net capital gain each year - even if nothing is distributed. This current inclusion eliminates the deferred gain that §1291 punishes. When the PFIC later distributes previously-taxed amounts, or when the shareholder sells, those amounts are tax-free (previously taxed income, or PTI).

The tradeoff: you pay tax every year on your share of the PFIC's income whether or not you receive cash. For a PFIC that retains most of its earnings (like a growing foreign fund), this creates a cash flow problem - tax due on phantom income. The QEF election also requires the PFIC to provide an annual PFIC statement under Treas. Reg. §1.1295-1(g) - many foreign funds will not provide this, making the QEF election impossible as a practical matter.

The QEF election must be made in the first year the corporation is a PFIC. If a US person holds a foreign fund for several years under the default §1291 regime and then tries to elect QEF, they cannot simply switch - they must first execute a "purging election" to clean up the §1291 taint on their existing shares. The purging election is itself a taxable event. This is why early identification of PFICs is critical.

Mark-to-Market Election: The Practical Alternative

For PFICs whose stock is marketable (listed on a national securities exchange or a foreign exchange meeting IRS standards), the mark-to-market election under IRC §1296 is often the most practical solution. Each year, the shareholder recognizes ordinary income or loss equal to the change in fair market value of the PFIC stock.

MTM gains are ordinary income - no preferential capital gains rates. MTM losses are ordinary losses, but only to the extent of prior MTM gains recognized (preventing the creation of net losses from PFIC investments). The MTM election eliminates the §1291 interest charge by ensuring there is no deferred gain to punish.

The MTM election is available for most shares of publicly traded foreign funds and ETFs held in brokerage accounts. It is not available for non-marketable PFICs (private foreign funds, foreign holding companies with no market for their shares).

The Once-a-PFIC-Always-a-PFIC Rule

IRC §1298(b)(1) provides that once a foreign corporation qualifies as a PFIC for any taxable year, it remains a PFIC with respect to a shareholder's shares even if it subsequently fails the income or asset tests in later years. The taint attaches to the shares, not just to the years of PFIC status.

This rule has significant implications for US persons who buy shares in a company that was a PFIC in its early years (common for startups that have passive income before generating operating revenue) but subsequently grows into an active business. The §1291 taint on the original shares persists unless the shareholder makes a purging election.

Start-up exception IRC §1298(b)(2). A foreign corporation is not treated as a PFIC for its first tax year if it is not a PFIC under a reasonable start-up exception and it is reasonably expected to not be a PFIC going forward. This exception is narrow and must be applied carefully with documentation.

The CFC/PFIC Overlap

A US shareholder who owns more than 50% of a controlled foreign corporation (CFC) is generally subject to Subpart F and NCTI inclusion rules rather than the PFIC rules. IRC §1297(d) provides that a foreign corporation is not treated as a PFIC with respect to a US person who is a CFC shareholder subject to Subpart F inclusions for the year. This overlap rule prevents double-counting, but it requires careful analysis when ownership thresholds change.

Form 8621: Filing Requirements

Form 8621 (Information Return by a Shareholder of a Passive Foreign Investment Company or Qualified Electing Fund) must be filed annually for each PFIC in which a US person has a direct or indirect interest. Filing is required even if there is no income or distribution in the year, though a simplified filing is available in certain circumstances.

Failure to file Form 8621 tolls the statute of limitations on the entire tax return - the IRS has unlimited time to assess additional tax for any year a required Form 8621 is not filed. This makes PFIC compliance non-optional even when the economic amounts are small.
