PFIC Rules: Excess Distribution, QEF & Mark-to-Market

IRC §1291-1298  •  Three Tax Regimes  •  Form 8621  •  Foreign Mutual Fund Trap  •  Updated 2026
IRC §1291 IRC §1295 (QEF) IRC §1296 (MTM) Form 8621
← cpavalidated.com

The passive foreign investment company rules are among the harshest anti-deferral regimes in the US tax code. A US person who holds stock in a PFIC without making a timely election faces the §1291 excess distribution regime - which taxes gains and distributions at the highest ordinary income rate plus an interest charge going back to the year of acquisition, eliminating any benefit of long-term capital gains rates. Foreign mutual funds, ETFs, and many foreign holding companies are PFICs. Most owners don't know it until it is too late.

The Core Problem

A US investor who holds a foreign mutual fund for 10 years and sells at a gain does not pay 20% capital gains tax. Under the §1291 default regime, the gain is allocated ratably over the holding period, taxed at the highest ordinary income rate for each prior year (currently 37%), and then subject to an interest charge as if the taxes had been owed and unpaid since each prior year. The effective combined rate routinely exceeds 50% and can approach 60% or more after interest. This is not a planning failure - it is the default rule.

What Is a PFIC? The Two Tests

A foreign corporation is a PFIC for a tax year if it meets either of two tests under IRC §1297:

Income Test
Asset Test
IRC §1297(a)(1)
75% or more of the corporation's gross income for the tax year is passive income (dividends, interest, rents, royalties, annuities, and gains from sale of passive assets)
IRC §1297(a)(2)
50% or more of the average value of the corporation's assets (determined quarterly) produce or are held for production of passive income

Either test alone is sufficient. A company that passes one test is a PFIC even if it fails the other. The tests are applied at the corporate level each year - a company can be a PFIC in some years and not others. A US shareholder who held stock during any PFIC year retains "PFIC taint" on those shares even if the company later ceases to be a PFIC.

The foreign mutual fund trap. Virtually every foreign-registered mutual fund, UCITS fund, ETF, and unit trust is a PFIC. These funds hold stocks, bonds, and cash - all passive assets - and generate dividends, interest, and capital gains - all passive income. A US person holding a British ISA, a Canadian mutual fund, an Irish UCITS ETF, or an Israeli provident fund almost certainly holds PFIC shares. The fact that the investment is labeled a "retirement account" or "pension" does not change the PFIC analysis unless a specific treaty provision or exclusion applies.

The Three PFIC Tax Regimes

A US shareholder of PFIC stock is subject to one of three tax regimes, depending on elections made:

1. Default Regime: IRC §1291 Excess Distribution

If no election is made, the §1291 regime applies automatically. It taxes two types of events: excess distributions received during the year, and gain recognized on disposition of PFIC stock.

An excess distribution is the amount by which actual distributions from the PFIC exceed 125% of the average distributions received over the prior 3 years (or the shareholder's holding period if shorter). The non-excess portion is taxed normally; the excess portion is subject to the harsh §1291 computation.

No capital gains rates under §1291. Even if the PFIC stock was held for decades and would otherwise qualify for the 0% or 20% long-term capital gains rate, the §1291 regime taxes the gain at ordinary rates. The preferential capital gains rates of IRC §1(h) do not apply to PFIC gain taxed under §1291. This is the primary punitive feature of the regime.

2. QEF Election: IRC §1295

A US shareholder can elect to treat a PFIC as a Qualified Electing Fund (QEF) by filing Form 8621 with the return for the first year of ownership (or for the first year the entity becomes a PFIC). Under the QEF election, the shareholder includes their pro-rata share of the PFIC's ordinary earnings and net capital gains in gross income each year - regardless of whether distributions are actually made.

The QEF election converts PFIC taxation to a pass-through-like regime: ordinary earnings are taxed as ordinary income, net capital gains are taxed at preferential capital gains rates. When actual distributions are made or stock is sold, previously included amounts are recovered tax-free (basis step-up). The QEF election is significantly more favorable than the §1291 default - but requires the PFIC to provide an annual information statement (PFIC Annual Information Statement) with the shareholder's share of ordinary earnings and net capital gains. Most publicly traded foreign funds do not provide this statement, making the QEF election practically unavailable.

3. Mark-to-Market Election: IRC §1296

For PFIC stock that is "marketable" (traded on a qualified exchange or national market system), a US shareholder can elect to mark the stock to market annually. Each year, the shareholder recognizes gain equal to the increase in value (ordinary income) or loss equal to the decrease in value (ordinary loss, limited to prior inclusions). On ultimate disposition, any remaining gain is ordinary income.

The MTM election eliminates the §1291 interest charge problem by treating gains as ordinary income each year rather than deferring them. The downside: all gains are ordinary income rather than capital gains, even if held long-term. For stock that appreciates steadily, the MTM election avoids the §1291 regime's interest charges at the cost of losing capital gains treatment.

Form 8621: Annual Reporting Requirement

Every US person who holds stock in a PFIC must file Form 8621 (Information Return by a Shareholder of a Passive Foreign Investment Company or Qualified Electing Fund) for each PFIC each year. The form must be filed even if no distribution was received and no election is being made - in that case, the shareholder checks the box indicating they are reporting as a §1291 fund.

The annual reporting requirement has no dollar threshold - a $500 foreign mutual fund and a $50 million PFIC portfolio both require Form 8621. The failure to file Form 8621 suspends the statute of limitations on the entire return for the year - not just the PFIC item. This is a significant compliance risk for taxpayers who have unknowingly held PFIC shares for years.

Statute of limitations suspension. IRC §1298(f) and the regulations require annual Form 8621 filing. If a required Form 8621 is not filed, the statute of limitations on assessment for that year's entire return remains open indefinitely - the IRS can assess additional tax at any time. This applies even if the PFIC item itself would otherwise be minor.

Purging Elections: Fixing Prior-Year PFIC Taint

A taxpayer who has held PFIC stock for several years without making an election can make a "purging election" to clear the prior §1291 taint and begin prospectively under QEF or MTM. The purging election treats the PFIC shares as sold at fair market value on the first day of the QEF or MTM election year. The deemed gain is taxed under §1291 (ordinary rates plus interest charge) - but this clears the taint so future appreciation is taxed more favorably.

The purging election is painful because it accelerates recognition of built-in gain under the worst possible regime. But for holdings expected to appreciate significantly, paying the §1291 tax now to start fresh under QEF or MTM is often the right decision.

The Indirect PFIC Problem: Look-Through Rules

PFIC status can also arise indirectly. Under IRC §1298(a), if a US person owns stock in a foreign corporation that itself owns 25% or more of another foreign corporation that is a PFIC, the US person is treated as owning a proportionate share of the PFIC's assets and income. This look-through rule means a foreign holding company that is not itself a PFIC can still expose its US shareholders to PFIC rules if it owns PFIC subsidiaries.

For pre-immigration planning, this rule is particularly important. A non-US person who owns shares in a foreign fund through a foreign holding company should restructure before becoming a US tax resident - once the substantial presence test or green card test is triggered, the PFIC rules apply retroactively to the holding period, not just from the date of US residency.
