401(k) Guide: Limits, Employer Match, Roth Option & Early Withdrawal

A 401(k) is the primary retirement savings vehicle for most American employees. The mechanics are straightforward - you defer salary into the plan before income tax, it grows tax-deferred, and you pay ordinary income tax when you withdraw in retirement. But there are contribution limits, matching rules, vesting schedules, a Roth option that flips the tax timing, and a 10% early withdrawal penalty with specific exceptions that every participant should understand.

2026 401(k) Contribution Limits

Employee elective deferral: $23,500

Age 50+ catch-up: $7,500 additional = $31,000 total

Super catch-up ages 60-63 (SECURE 2.0): $11,250 additional = $34,750 total

Total annual additions (employee + employer): $70,000 limit (§415(c))

Compensation limit: $350,000 (only compensation up to this counts for plan purposes)

High earners should not ignore the Roth 401(k) just because they are in a high bracket now. Unlike the Roth IRA, there is no income limit on Roth 401(k) contributions. If you expect rates to rise, or want tax diversification in retirement, or want to leave tax-free assets to heirs, the Roth 401(k) is a powerful tool available to any W-2 employee regardless of income. SECURE 2.0 eliminated RMDs from Roth 401(k) accounts starting 2024, which makes them even more attractive for long-term accumulation.

Employer Match: Free Money with Conditions

Most employers match a portion of employee contributions - typically 50% or 100% of the first 3%-6% of compensation deferred. Always contribute at least enough to capture the full employer match - it is an immediate 50%-100% return on that portion of your contribution before any investment gains. The employer match is always pre-tax, even if your own contributions are Roth.

Vesting: Employer match dollars are subject to a vesting schedule - you own them fully only after working for the employer for a specified period. Common schedules: cliff vesting (100% after 3 years), graded vesting (20% per year over 6 years). Employee contributions are always 100% immediately vested.

Early Withdrawal: The 10% Penalty and Its Exceptions

Distributions before age 59.5 are subject to a 10% early withdrawal penalty under IRC §72(t), in addition to ordinary income tax on the full amount. This combination - often 37% income tax + 10% penalty = 47% total cost - makes early withdrawal extremely expensive. However, §72(t) includes specific exceptions where the 10% penalty does not apply:

  • Separation from service at age 55 or later (55 rule - only for the plan of the employer you just left)
  • Substantially equal periodic payments (SEPP / 72(t) distributions)
  • Total and permanent disability
  • Death (distributions to beneficiaries)
  • Qualified domestic relations order (QDRO - divorce)
  • Medical expenses exceeding 7.5% of AGI
  • Birth or adoption - up to $5,000 (SECURE Act)
  • Terminal illness (SECURE 2.0)
  • Domestic abuse victim distributions up to $10,000 (SECURE 2.0)

401(k) Loans: Not a Free Pass

Many plans allow loans up to 50% of the vested account balance or $50,000, whichever is less. The loan is not taxable when taken and is repaid with interest back into your own account. But if you leave your employer while a loan is outstanding, the balance becomes due quickly - often within 60-90 days - and any unpaid balance is treated as a taxable distribution subject to the 10% penalty if you are under 59.5. Loans taken just before a job change or layoff are a common source of unplanned tax bills.

401(k) hardship withdrawals are taxable and penalized - they are not the same as loans. A hardship withdrawal (for immediate and heavy financial need) is a permanent distribution, not repaid, and is fully taxable plus the 10% penalty unless an exception applies. The plan may also prohibit new contributions for 6 months after a hardship withdrawal in some older plan designs. Exhaust other options before taking a hardship withdrawal.
