Required Minimum Distributions: Age 73, Inherited IRAs & QCDs

RMD Age 73 (SECURE 2.0)  •  Uniform Lifetime Table  •  Inherited IRA 10-Year Rule  •  QCD $108,000  •  Updated 2026
IRC §401(a)(9) SECURE 2.0 P.L. 117-328 IRC §4974
← Retirement & Savings

Required minimum distributions are mandatory annual withdrawals from tax-deferred retirement accounts beginning at age 73 under SECURE 2.0. Missing an RMD triggers a 25% excise tax on the shortfall, reduced to 10% if corrected within two years. The inherited IRA rules were completely restructured by SECURE Act 1.0 in 2019, eliminating the stretch IRA for most non-spouse beneficiaries. Understanding who must take RMDs, how to calculate them, and how to minimize their tax impact is essential planning for every retiree and their advisors.

2026 Key RMD Numbers

RMD starting age: 73 - for anyone who turned 72 after December 31, 2022. Age rises to 75 starting in 2033.

First RMD deadline: April 1 of the year following the year you turn 73. All subsequent RMDs: December 31 each year. Taking the first RMD in the following April means two RMDs in that year - both are taxable income.

Missed RMD penalty: 25% excise tax on the shortfall (IRC §4974). Reduced to 10% if corrected within the 2-year correction window under SECURE 2.0.

QCD limit: $108,000 per year (2026, indexed). Excluded from gross income, counts toward RMD. Must go directly to a public charity - not to a donor-advised fund.

Roth IRA: No RMDs during the owner's lifetime. Roth 401(k)s: no RMDs after 2024 (SECURE 2.0).

The RMD Calculation: Uniform Lifetime Table

Each year's RMD is computed by dividing the prior December 31 account balance by the applicable distribution period from the IRS Uniform Lifetime Table (Treas. Reg. §1.401(a)(9)-9). The table was updated in 2022 to reflect longer life expectancies - distribution periods are longer, meaning required distributions are slightly smaller.

Example: Owner turns 75 in 2026. IRA balance December 31, 2025 = $800,000. Distribution period at age 75 per the Uniform Lifetime Table = 24.6. RMD for 2026 = $800,000 / 24.6 = $32,520.

The only exception to the Uniform Lifetime Table is when the sole beneficiary is a spouse more than 10 years younger - the Joint Life and Last Survivor Table applies, producing a longer distribution period and lower RMD.

Inherited IRA: The 10-Year Rule

SECURE Act 1.0 (P.L. 116-94, effective for deaths after December 31, 2019) eliminated the stretch IRA for most non-spouse beneficiaries. Most beneficiaries must now fully distribute the inherited IRA within 10 years of the original owner's death, with no required annual withdrawals within the 10-year period - unless the owner had already begun RMDs.

If the Owner Died Before Their Required Beginning Date

No annual RMDs are required within the 10-year period. The beneficiary may take any amount at any time, as long as the account is fully depleted by December 31 of the 10th year following the owner's death. A beneficiary could take nothing for 9 years and withdraw the entire balance in year 10.

If the Owner Died On or After Their Required Beginning Date

Annual RMDs are required in years 1 through 9, calculated using the beneficiary's single life expectancy. The account must still be fully depleted by the end of year 10.

Eligible Designated Beneficiaries - Stretch Still Available

The 10-year rule does not apply to Eligible Designated Beneficiaries (EDBs), who may still use the life expectancy stretch method: surviving spouses (who may also roll over to their own IRA), minor children of the deceased until age 21 (then 10-year rule kicks in), disabled individuals under IRC §72(m)(7), chronically ill individuals, and individuals not more than 10 years younger than the deceased owner.

Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCDs)

A QCD allows an IRA owner age 70½ or older to transfer up to $108,000 per year (2026, indexed for inflation) directly from the IRA to a qualified public charity. The transfer is excluded from gross income - it does not count as income and no charitable deduction is claimed. The QCD counts toward the RMD for the year, making it one of the most tax-efficient strategies for charitably inclined retirees.

QCDs are especially valuable for non-itemizers. After TCJA roughly doubled the standard deduction, most retirees no longer itemize. A charitable cash contribution from personal funds produces no deduction for non-itemizers. A QCD produces the same economic result while reducing gross income directly - which also reduces adjusted gross income, potentially lowering Medicare Part B and D premiums (IRMAA) and keeping more Social Security income tax-free. The QCD cannot go to a donor-advised fund, supporting organization, or private foundation.
