RMD Calculation Guide: Uniform Lifetime Table & SECURE 2.0

Starting Age 73  •  Uniform Lifetime Table  •  First-Year Election  •  Multiple IRA Aggregation  •  Inherited IRA Annual RMDs  •  Updated 2026
IRC §401(a)(9) Treas. Reg. §1.401(a)(9)-5 SECURE 2.0 Act
← Retirement & Savings

Required minimum distributions force taxable withdrawals from tax-deferred retirement accounts starting at age 73, ensuring the government eventually collects tax on deferred savings. The calculation is straightforward - prior year account balance divided by a life expectancy factor from the IRS Uniform Lifetime Table - but the mechanics around the first RMD, multiple accounts, inherited IRAs, and the interaction with Roth conversions require careful planning.

Key RMD Numbers for 2026

RMD starting age: 73 (for individuals born 1951-1959); 75 for those born after 1959 (SECURE 2.0)

Required beginning date: April 1 of the year after reaching age 73

Measurement date for account balance: December 31 of the prior year

Penalty for missing an RMD: 25% excise tax on the amount not distributed (reduced from 50% by SECURE 2.0); reducible to 10% if corrected within 2 years

QCD limit: $108,000 per taxpayer per year - counts toward RMD but excluded from income

The RMD Formula

Annual RMD Calculation
RMD= Account Balance (December 31 prior year)
÷Distribution PeriodLife expectancy factor from the applicable IRS table - based on age as of December 31 of the distribution year

The distribution period comes from one of three IRS tables in Treas. Reg. §1.401(a)(9)-9, Appendix B:

  • Uniform Lifetime Table - used by virtually all IRA owners whose sole beneficiary is not a spouse more than 10 years younger
  • Joint and Last Survivor Table - used only when the sole beneficiary is a spouse more than 10 years younger; produces a longer distribution period (smaller RMD)
  • Single Life Expectancy Table - used for inherited IRAs by eligible designated beneficiaries who elect the life expectancy method

First-Year RMD: The April 1 Election

The first RMD must be taken by April 1 of the year following the year in which the account owner reaches age 73. All subsequent RMDs must be taken by December 31 of each year. This creates an important choice: a person who turns 73 in 2026 can take their first RMD by April 1, 2027, or take it during 2026.

Taking two RMDs in one year stacks income. If you defer the first RMD to April 1 of the following year, you must also take the second RMD (for that year) by December 31 of the same year. Two RMDs in one calendar year means two RMDs of ordinary income in one year - potentially pushing you into a higher bracket, triggering IRMAA surcharges, or making more Social Security taxable. In most cases, taking the first RMD in the year you turn 73 (rather than deferring to April 1) is the better choice. Run the numbers both ways.

Multiple IRAs: Aggregation Rules

When a taxpayer owns multiple traditional IRAs, the RMD is calculated separately for each account (using each account's December 31 balance and the same distribution period), but the total RMD can be withdrawn from any one or combination of the IRAs. The taxpayer is not required to take a proportional amount from each account.

The aggregation rules differ by account type:

Inherited IRA Annual RMDs Under the 10-Year Rule

IRS final regulations (2024) confirmed that non-eligible designated beneficiaries who inherit an IRA from an owner who had already reached their required beginning date must take annual RMDs in years 1-9 of the 10-year period and distribute the remainder by December 31 of year 10. The annual RMD for an inherited IRA is calculated using the beneficiary's own life expectancy from the Single Life Expectancy Table, not the Uniform Lifetime Table.

The required beginning date matters for inherited IRA treatment:

  • Decedent died before required beginning date: No annual RMDs required during years 1-9. Full account must be distributed by December 31 of year 10.
  • Decedent died on or after required beginning date: Annual RMDs required in years 1-9, using the beneficiary's Single Life Expectancy factor. Full account by December 31 of year 10.
The "eligible designated beneficiary" exception allows lifetime stretch. Surviving spouses, disabled or chronically ill individuals, individuals not more than 10 years younger than the decedent, and minor children of the account owner (until majority) are eligible designated beneficiaries and can elect to stretch distributions over their own lifetime using the Single Life Expectancy Table - not subject to the 10-year rule.

QCDs: The Tax-Free RMD Alternative

Taxpayers aged 70.5 or older can direct up to $108,000 (2026) per year from a traditional IRA directly to a qualifying charity as a qualified charitable distribution (QCD). The QCD counts toward the RMD for the year but is excluded from gross income. For taxpayers who don't itemize deductions, a QCD is the only mechanism that makes charitable giving tax-efficient in retirement - the income is excluded from AGI entirely rather than just deducted from taxable income.

A QCD must go directly from the IRA custodian to the charity - the taxpayer cannot take a distribution and then donate the proceeds. The distribution must be made to a public 501(c)(3) charity; donor-advised funds and private foundations do not qualify.
