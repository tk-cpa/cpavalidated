Roth Conversion: Tax Cost, Pro-Rata Rule & Timing Strategy

A Roth conversion moves money from a traditional IRA (or other pre-tax retirement account) into a Roth IRA. The converted amount is includible in gross income as ordinary income in the year of conversion - no special capital gains rates, no deferral. The converted funds then grow tax-free in the Roth and qualified distributions are tax-free for life. The question is always timing: converting in a year when ordinary income is low - a gap year, early retirement before Social Security and RMDs begin, or a year with large deductions - minimizes the tax cost and maximizes the benefit of the conversion.

The Roth Conversion Math

Break-even logic: A Roth conversion makes mathematical sense when your current marginal rate is equal to or lower than your expected rate at distribution. If you convert at 22% today and would otherwise distribute at 32% in retirement, the conversion saves 10 percentage points on the converted amount. If rates are reversed, the conversion costs more than it saves.

Beyond break-even: Even when rates are similar, Roth conversions provide: no required minimum distributions (no RMDs on Roth IRAs during the owner's lifetime); tax-free growth on all future earnings; a tax-free inheritance for beneficiaries; and estate tax reduction by pre-paying income tax now.

The optimal window: The years between retirement and age 73 (when RMDs begin) and before Social Security claiming are often the lowest-income years of a retiree's life. Converting in this window fills low brackets cheaply before RMDs and Social Security force income higher.

The Pro-Rata Rule: All IRAs Count

If you have made nondeductible contributions to a traditional IRA (tracked on Form 8606), you have basis in your IRA - after-tax money that should not be taxed again on distribution. The pro-rata rule calculates what percentage of your IRA is basis by dividing total basis by the total value of ALL traditional, SEP, and SIMPLE IRAs you own (not Roth IRAs). You cannot selectively convert only the after-tax basis; the conversion must be pro-rated across basis and pre-tax funds.

Example: You have $100,000 in a traditional IRA with $10,000 of nondeductible basis. You convert $20,000 to Roth. Pro-rata basis = 10%. The tax-free portion is $2,000 (10% of $20,000). The taxable portion is $18,000 - even though the $10,000 of basis was already taxed. The basis carries over to the remaining traditional IRA and reduces future distributions.

IRMAA and ACA Premium Credit Impacts

A large Roth conversion increases MAGI, which can trigger Medicare premium surcharges (IRMAA) in the second year following the conversion year. It can also reduce or eliminate ACA premium tax credits for pre-Medicare retirees who buy coverage on the exchange. Model the IRMAA brackets and ACA cliff before executing a large conversion in a single year. Partial conversions spread over multiple years avoid spikes that cross these thresholds.

The Conversion Ladder Strategy

Rather than converting a large amount in one year, a conversion ladder converts enough each year to fill a target tax bracket - typically the top of the 22% or 24% bracket - over multiple years. This smooths the tax cost, avoids IRMAA spikes, and systematically reduces the pre-tax IRA balance before RMDs begin. A married couple retiring at 62 with 11 years before RMDs can potentially convert the entire IRA balance at rates well below what RMD-forced distributions would cost.
