SALT Deduction Planning: $40K Cap & PTET Elections

IRC §164  •  OBBBA Permanent Cap  •  Pass-Through Entity Tax Workaround  •  Updated 2026
IRC §164(b)(6) OBBBA P.L. 119-21 PTET Elections
← cpavalidated.com

The OBBBA permanently set the state and local tax deduction cap at $40,000 for most taxpayers, up from the TCJA's $10,000. For high earners the cap still bites hard. The primary legal workaround - the pass-through entity tax (PTET) election - remains intact and is now more valuable than ever. Understanding how PTET works, which states offer it, and when it makes sense is the core of SALT planning in 2026.

The Rule - IRC §164(b)(6) as amended by OBBBA

For tax years beginning after December 31, 2025, the deduction for state and local taxes paid is limited to $40,000 per return ($20,000 for married filing separately). This applies to the aggregate of state and local income taxes, general sales taxes, and real property taxes claimed on Schedule A. The cap is permanent - it does not sunset.

An income-based phaseout applies for taxpayers with adjusted gross income above $500,000 (MFJ) / $400,000 (single) - the cap reduces by $200 for every $1,000 of AGI above the threshold, down to a floor of $10,000.

The Phaseout: High Earners Still Face a Lower Cap

The $40,000 cap applies in full only up to the AGI phaseout thresholds. Above those thresholds, the cap decreases. At high enough income levels, the effective cap falls back toward $10,000 - making PTET planning even more critical for the highest earners.

MFS trap. Married filing separately taxpayers get a $20,000 base cap with a $400,000 AGI threshold. Splitting returns to double the cap does not work - the MFS cap is half the MFJ cap, not equal to it. In most cases MFS produces a worse overall tax result when SALT is the only consideration.

The PTET Election: The Main Workaround

The pass-through entity tax election allows partnerships, S-corporations, and in some states LLCs to pay state income tax at the entity level rather than the individual level. The entity deducts the state tax payment as a business expense - reducing federal ordinary income before it flows to the individual owner's K-1. The individual then claims a credit on their state return for the state tax paid by the entity on their behalf.

The result: state income taxes that would have been capped at $40,000 (or less under the phaseout) on the individual's Schedule A instead reduce federal taxable income dollar-for-dollar as a business deduction. The IRS blessed this structure in Notice 2020-75, and Congress left it intact under OBBBA.

IRS Notice 2020-75 - The Legal Foundation

The IRS confirmed that a state or local income tax imposed on and paid by a partnership or S-corporation on its income is a deductible tax at the entity level under IRC §164. The deduction reduces the entity's income before allocation to partners or shareholders. The $10,000 (now $40,000) SALT cap applies to taxes paid by the individual - not to taxes paid by the entity. This is the mechanism that makes PTET elections work.

State PTET Elections: Who Offers It and Key Terms

As of 2026, over 35 states have enacted PTET legislation. The rules vary significantly by state - some are elective, some are mandatory for certain entities, credit mechanisms differ, and not all states allow the credit to flow to nonresident owners cleanly. Here are the major states:

PTET Planning Considerations

The Estimated Payment Timing Problem

Most state PTET regimes require the entity to make estimated tax payments during the year for the deduction to be claimed in that year. A cash-basis entity that waits until March 15 to pay the PTET for the prior year deducts it in the year paid - not the year the income was earned. Missing estimated payment deadlines can shift the deduction to a later year, disrupting the planning.

Nonresident Owner Complications

When an entity has owners in multiple states, the PTET credit must flow to the correct state return for each owner. A New York PTET payment credits against each owner's New York nonresident return - but an owner who lives in California and works through a New York partnership may receive only a partial benefit if New York's credit does not fully offset their California tax on the same income. Composite returns and withholding requirements add further complexity.

When PTET Does Not Help

  • C-corporations: not eligible - they already deduct state income taxes at the entity level
  • Sole proprietors and single-member LLCs taxed as disregarded entities: no entity-level election available
  • Taxpayers in no-income-tax states: no state tax to shift
  • Taxpayers subject to AMT: the SALT deduction was already disallowed for AMT purposes under prior law; the PTET entity-level deduction reduces AMT income indirectly through the K-1 but the interaction is complex
  • Entities with significant losses: the PTET election creates a state tax payment when there may be no net benefit if the entity is already in a loss position

Real Property Tax: Still Capped, No Workaround

Property taxes paid on a personal residence are subject to the SALT cap with no equivalent workaround. The PTET structure applies only to income taxes paid by pass-through entities. A taxpayer paying $30,000 in New York City property taxes and $50,000 in state income taxes through a PTET election would fully benefit from the PTET on the income tax portion but still face the cap on the property taxes - and the aggregate cap applies to both together.

For investment real estate held in a partnership or LLC, property taxes are deductible at the entity level as a business expense (IRC §162 or §212) and are not subject to the SALT cap - the cap applies only to taxes reported on Schedule A. This is an important distinction for real estate investors.

Other Planning Strategies

Accelerating deductible SALT into years below the phaseout threshold. In years where AGI happens to be lower (sale of a business, retirement, sabbatical), paying additional state estimated taxes or property taxes before year-end to use more of the cap is a timing strategy worth modeling.

Charitable contribution in lieu of taxes. Some states allow taxpayers to make contributions to state-designated funds in exchange for state tax credits. The IRS significantly curtailed these arrangements under Treas. Reg. §1.170A-1(h)(3) (the quid pro quo reduction rules), limiting the charitable deduction to the excess of the contribution over the state credit received. These structures have limited utility post-OBBBA but are worth understanding.

Business entity structure review. Taxpayers currently operating as sole proprietors or single-member LLCs who have significant state income tax exposure should model whether converting to a multi-member entity (partnership or S-corp) opens the door to a PTET election in their state. The conversion has non-tax implications that must be weighed separately.
