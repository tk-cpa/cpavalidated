SALT Planning: $10,000 Cap, PTET Elections & State Workarounds

$10K Cap Permanent Under OBBBA • PTET in 36+ States • IRS Notice 2020-75 OK • Entity-Level Deduction
IRC §164(b)(6)IRS Notice 2020-75OBBBA P.L. 119-21
← State & Compliance

The $10,000 SALT deduction cap - enacted by TCJA in 2017 and made permanent by OBBBA - limits the federal itemized deduction for state income taxes, property taxes, and sales taxes to $10,000 per return ($5,000 MFS). High-earners in high-tax states (California, New York, New Jersey, Illinois, Massachusetts) routinely pay $50,000 to $200,000+ in state and local taxes annually, receiving a deduction for only the first $10,000. The pass-through entity tax workaround, now available in more than 36 states, allows business owners who receive income through partnerships or S-corporations to deduct their state taxes at the entity level - an above-the-line deduction that is not subject to the individual $10,000 SALT cap.

How the PTET Workaround Works

Without PTET: Partnership earns $1,000,000 and passes through to a New York resident partner. Partner pays NY state income tax of approximately $109,700 (10.9% rate). Federal deduction limited to $10,000 (SALT cap). The partner gets no federal deduction for $99,700 of state tax paid.

With NY PTET election: Partnership elects to pay the $109,700 NY PTE tax at the entity level. Partnership deducts $109,700 as a business expense on the partnership return. The federal deduction flows through to the partner as a reduction in ordinary income - fully deductible, not subject to the $10,000 individual SALT cap. Partner receives a NY state tax credit equal to the state tax paid, making the election revenue-neutral for the state.

IRS blessing: IRS Notice 2020-75 confirmed that entity-level state income taxes paid by partnerships and S-corps are deductible by the entity, and the $10,000 individual SALT cap does not apply to entity-level taxes. The notice was a green light for state PTET programs.

PTET Election Mechanics and Timing

PTET elections must be made annually by a specified deadline that varies by state. New York requires the election to be made by March 15 of the tax year. California requires it by June 15. Most states require the election to be made during the tax year - a retroactive election is not possible. The payment of the estimated PTET tax is also typically due during the tax year to generate the federal deduction in that year rather than the following year. Missing the election or payment deadline means losing the SALT cap workaround for the entire year.

The PTET election is not always beneficial. States with lower income tax rates, partners/shareholders in lower federal brackets, or entities with significant non-resident owners require careful analysis. The benefit depends on the difference between the partner's federal marginal rate and the state rate - and whether the partner can fully utilize the state tax credit against their personal state return. A partner who cannot fully use the state credit gets no benefit from the PTET.

Property Taxes and the $10,000 Cap

Real property taxes on a personal residence are subject to the same $10,000 combined SALT cap. High-end homeowners in New York, New Jersey, Connecticut, and Illinois routinely pay $20,000 to $80,000 in annual property taxes - receiving a federal deduction for only $10,000 of that amount. The PTET workaround does not help with personal property taxes or real estate taxes on personal residences - only with state income taxes paid through a business entity.
