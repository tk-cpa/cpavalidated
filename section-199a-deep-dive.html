§199A QBI Deduction Deep Dive: SSTB, W-2 Wage Limits & Aggregation

SSTB Classification by Profession  •  W-2 Wage Limitation  •  UBIA Alternative  •  Aggregation Elections  •  Rental Safe Harbor  •  2026
IRC §199A Treas. Reg. §1.199A OBBBA Permanent
← Business & Entity

The §199A QBI deduction is 20% of qualified business income from pass-through entities and sole proprietorships - but the 20% figure is the ceiling, not the floor, for higher-income taxpayers. Above the threshold ($394,600 MFJ / $197,300 single for 2026), the deduction is limited by either the W-2 wage test or W-2 wages plus UBIA of qualified property. For specified service trades or businesses (SSTBs), the deduction phases out entirely in a $100 income range above the threshold. Understanding which businesses are SSTBs and how the wage limitation works is essential for any pass-through owner above the threshold.

2026 §199A Thresholds - OBBBA Permanent

Below threshold: $197,300 (single) / $394,600 (MFJ). Full 20% deduction with no wage limitation. SSTBs fully qualify below threshold.

Phase-in range (SSTB phase-out + wage limitation phase-in): $100 range above threshold. Both limitations apply proportionally.

Above phase-in: $297,300 (single) / $494,600 (MFJ). Full W-2 wage limitation applies. SSTBs completely disqualified.

OBBBA P.L. 119-21 made §199A permanent with no sunset date.

The W-2 Wage Limitation: Above-Threshold Mechanics

Above the threshold, the §199A deduction for each business is limited to the greater of:

W-2 Wage Limitation (above threshold)
Option 1:50% of W-2 wagesWages paid by the qualified trade or business
Option 2:25% of W-2 wagesPLUS
+2.5% of UBIAUnadjusted basis immediately after acquisition of all qualified property
Usewhichever is GREATERThe deduction cannot exceed the lesser of: 20% of QBI or the applicable wage limitation

W-2 wages for §199A purposes means wages paid by the trade or business that are properly allocable to QBI. This includes wages on Box 1, elective deferrals (Box 12), and deferred compensation - but not guaranteed payments to partners, which are not wages. For S-corps, the owner's reasonable salary is included in W-2 wages.

UBIA is the unadjusted basis of qualified property immediately after acquisition - essentially the original purchase price of depreciable assets used in the business. This option primarily benefits capital-intensive businesses (manufacturing, real estate) with large asset bases but modest payrolls.

SSTB Classification: Which Professions Are Excluded

IRC §199A(d)(1)(A) defines a Specified Service Trade or Business as any trade or business involving the performance of services in the following fields. Above the income threshold, SSTBs are completely excluded from the §199A deduction.

FieldSSTB?Key Nuance
HealthYesPhysicians, surgeons, dentists, nurses, chiropractors, pharmacists, therapists. Does NOT include veterinarians per final regs.
LawYesAttorneys, paralegals, legal consultants providing legal services. Does not include court reporting.
AccountingYesCPAs, bookkeepers, tax preparers, financial statement preparation. Does NOT include payment processing or payroll services.
Actuarial scienceYesActuaries providing actuarial services.
Performing artsYesMusicians, actors, entertainers performing services directly. Does not include those providing behind-the-scenes services.
ConsultingYesProviding professional advice and counsel. Does NOT include consulting embedded in product sales.
AthleticsYesAthletes, sports coaches, team managers performing services directly.
Financial servicesYesManaging wealth, advising on investments, financial planning. Does NOT include banking, mortgage brokers, insurance agents.
Brokerage servicesYesArranging transactions between buyers and sellers for a commission. Does NOT include real estate agents per regs (real estate brokerage is NOT an SSTB).
Engineering / architectureNoExplicitly excluded from SSTB definition by statute.
Real estateNoRental income, property management, real estate development - not SSTB. Real estate agents - not SSTB per regs.
Manufacturing / retail / technologyNoProduct-based businesses generally not SSTB unless primarily providing services using reputation/skill.
"Reputation or skill" catch-allYes (narrow)Businesses where the principal asset is the reputation/skill of one or more employees/owners - interpreted narrowly to cover celebrity endorsements and speaking fees.
The de minimis rule: mixed businesses. If an entity has SSTB and non-SSTB income, it is treated as entirely non-SSTB if SSTB gross receipts are less than 10% of total gross receipts (or less than 5% if gross receipts exceed $25 million). Above those thresholds, the business must separate SSTB and non-SSTB activities. Treas. Reg. §1.199A-5(c)(1) governs this analysis.

Aggregation Elections

Taxpayers can elect to aggregate multiple businesses for §199A purposes if they meet common ownership (50%+ direct or indirect ownership) and at least two of the following conditions: same customers, same products/services, same facilities, same employees/administration, or same management. Aggregation is beneficial when combining a high-wage business (which passes the wage test easily) with a high-income business (which would otherwise be limited).

Aggregation example. A physician (SSTB, above threshold, $0 deduction) owns 60% of a medical device company (non-SSTB, $2M QBI, $50K wages). The SSTB cannot be aggregated with the non-SSTB to inflate wages. But if the physician also owns 60% of a practice management company (non-SSTB, $100K QBI, $200K wages), the two non-SSTB entities CAN be aggregated - the $200K wages support the deduction on combined $2.1M QBI. The SSTB remains separate and disqualified. Aggregation elections are made annually on the return and must be consistently applied going forward.

Rental Activity Safe Harbor

Rev. Proc. 2019-38 provides a safe harbor under which a rental real estate enterprise is treated as a trade or business for §199A purposes if: (1) separate books and records are maintained, (2) 250+ hours of rental services are performed annually, and (3) contemporaneous records of services are kept. Self-rentals (renting to a commonly-controlled trade or business) automatically qualify without meeting the safe harbor, as do triple-net leases under certain conditions.
