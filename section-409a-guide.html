§409A Nonqualified Deferred Compensation: Rules & Penalties

Six Permissible Triggers • Deferral Election Timing • 20% Excise Tax • Short-Term Deferral Exception
IRC §409ATreas. Reg. §1.409A-1Notice 2010-6
← Individual Tax

IRC §409A governs essentially all arrangements under which compensation earned in one year is paid in a later year - nonqualified deferred compensation, severance agreements, equity compensation with deferred features, and bonus plans with delayed payment. Getting §409A wrong is expensive: the deferred amount is included in income immediately, a 20% excise tax is imposed on top of the regular income tax, and premium interest accrues from the year of deferral. Many compensation arrangements that companies and employees do not think of as "deferred compensation" are caught by §409A's broad definition.

§409A at a Glance

Who it covers: Any arrangement under which a service provider earns compensation in one year but receives it in a later year - employees, independent contractors, directors.

Core requirement: The plan must specify at the time of the deferral election (1) the amount to be deferred, (2) the time of payment, and (3) the form of payment. Payment may only occur on one of six permissible triggers.

Penalty for violation: Immediate income inclusion of the entire deferred amount, 20% additional excise tax under §409A(a)(1)(B), and premium interest at the federal underpayment rate plus 1%.

What §409A Covers: Broader Than Most People Think

The §409A definition of nonqualified deferred compensation is intentionally broad. It covers: traditional deferred compensation plans where executives elect to defer salary or bonus; severance agreements that pay more than the §409A separation pay exception allows; equity grants with below-market exercise prices; stock appreciation rights (SARs) that are not settled within the short-term deferral window; certain bonus plans where payment is contingent on future service; employment agreements with change-in-control payment provisions; and consulting agreements with deferred payment features.

Key exclusions from §409A: qualified plans (§401(k), pension, ESOP), §403(b) plans, §457(b) eligible governmental plans, short-term deferrals paid within 2.5 months after the year of vesting, separation pay within the §409A safe harbor limits, and bona fide vacation/sick leave accruals.

Stock options and SARs granted at fair market value are excluded from §409A - but options or SARs granted below fair market value are fully subject to it. This is why §409A valuation (the "409A valuation" that startups obtain before each equity grant) is not optional for private companies. If a company grants options at a strike price below FMV and the IRS determines the strike price was below FMV, the entire spread becomes a §409A violation - immediate ordinary income plus 20% excise tax on the employee at vesting, with no correction available after the fact.

The Six Permissible Payment Triggers

Under §409A(a)(2), deferred compensation may only be paid upon one or more of the following events:

  • Separation from service: The most common trigger. For "specified employees" (top-50 officers of public companies), payment must be delayed six months after separation.
  • Disability: As defined in §409A - the employee is unable to engage in any substantial gainful activity for at least 12 months, or is receiving disability benefits under Social Security or the company's long-term disability plan.
  • Death.
  • Change in control: As specifically defined under §409A regulations - not any change of control definition in a contract, but the IRC §409A definition involving 30% ownership thresholds and Board composition tests.
  • Unforeseeable emergency: Severe financial hardship caused by illness, casualty loss, or other extraordinary circumstance beyond the employee's control. A narrow exception.
  • Fixed time or fixed schedule: Payment on a specified date or per a specified schedule established at the time of deferral.
You cannot accelerate a §409A payment and you cannot further defer it without following strict procedures. If a plan says "pay at age 65," paying at age 63 because the employee retires is a §409A violation unless separation from service is also a specified trigger. If a plan says "pay upon separation from service," extending the payment date requires a subsequent deferral election made at least 12 months before the original payment date, deferring the payment at least five years further. Accelerations and improper elections are the most common §409A violations in practice.

Deferral Election Timing

For recurring deferrals, the election must be made before the beginning of the year in which the services creating the right to the compensation are performed - generally December 31 for a January 1 effective date. For performance-based compensation meeting specific criteria, the election deadline extends to six months before the end of the performance period. For new participants, the initial deferral election may be made within 30 days of first becoming eligible to participate - but only for compensation earned after the election.

Correction Programs

The IRS has issued correction programs under Notice 2010-6 and Notice 2010-80 that allow certain §409A documentary failures (plan language violations without an operational failure) to be corrected with reduced or no tax consequences. Operational failures - situations where a payment was actually made or withheld at the wrong time - have limited correction paths with partial relief available under Notice 2010-80 if corrected promptly in the same or following year. There is no voluntary disclosure program that fully eliminates §409A penalties once an operational failure has occurred.
