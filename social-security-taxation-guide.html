Social Security Taxation: 85% Inclusion & Provisional Income

Provisional Income Calculation  •  50% & 85% Thresholds  •  Reduction Strategies  •  IRMAA Interaction  •  Updated 2026
IRC §86 Provisional Income IRMAA
← cpavalidated.com

Up to 85% of Social Security benefits can be included in taxable income - but only if the recipient's "provisional income" exceeds certain thresholds. These thresholds have never been indexed for inflation since they were set in 1983 and 1993, meaning more retirees fall into the taxable range every year. Understanding how provisional income works and how to manage it is essential retirement tax planning, especially because the same income that triggers Social Security taxation also triggers IRMAA Medicare premium surcharges.

The Two Thresholds - IRC §86

First threshold (50% inclusion): Single $25,000 / MFJ $32,000 - above this, 50% of SS benefits or 50% of the excess of provisional income over the threshold, whichever is less, is included in income.

Second threshold (85% inclusion): Single $34,000 / MFJ $44,000 - above this, up to 85% of SS benefits are included in taxable income. These thresholds have never been adjusted for inflation since they were enacted.

The maximum taxable amount is always 85% of total Social Security benefits received - never 100%.

Tax-exempt interest counts. This is the most surprising element of the provisional income calculation. Municipal bond interest is excluded from regular AGI - but it is added back for purposes of determining how much Social Security is taxable. A retiree who holds large municipal bond positions to "avoid taxes" may find that those bond proceeds are making their Social Security taxable - creating a situation where the after-tax yield on munis is worse than expected.

The Torpedo Zone: The Hidden Marginal Rate

In the phase-in ranges (between the first and second thresholds), every additional dollar of income causes $1.50 of taxable income - $1.00 of the new income plus $0.50 of newly taxable Social Security. This creates an effective marginal rate of 1.5x the regular rate. At the 22% bracket, the effective marginal rate in this zone is 33%. At the 12% bracket, it's 18%.

This "torpedo" effect makes income management in retirement particularly important. An extra $1,000 IRA withdrawal in the torpedo zone creates $1,500 of taxable income - the $1,000 withdrawal plus $500 of newly taxable Social Security. Above the 85% threshold, the ratio returns to 1:1, and the torpedo effect disappears.

Strategies to Reduce Social Security Taxation

Roth conversions before Social Security begins. Converting traditional IRA balances to Roth during the years between retirement and age 70 (when SS benefits start) reduces future RMDs - which are one of the primary drivers of high provisional income. Lower RMDs in future years mean lower provisional income and less Social Security taxation. The Roth conversion itself creates income in the conversion year, but the permanent reduction in future RMDs can be worth more.

Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCDs). QCDs up to $111,000 per year from IRAs (age 70.5+) reduce AGI dollar for dollar - unlike charitable deductions which only reduce taxable income. Reducing AGI reduces provisional income, which can reduce the taxable portion of Social Security. For retirees who donate to charity, QCDs are almost always superior to taking the RMD and then donating cash.

Capital gain harvesting at 0% rates. Retirees in the 12% ordinary income bracket may qualify for the 0% long-term capital gains rate (income below approximately $47,025 single / $94,050 MFJ in 2026). Strategically realizing capital gains in low-income years steps up the basis of appreciated positions at zero capital gains tax - but be careful: those gains still count as provisional income and may make more Social Security taxable in that year.

Avoid municipal bonds if in the torpedo zone. As noted above, tax-exempt interest counts toward provisional income. For retirees whose provisional income is in the phase-in range, municipal bonds are often not the optimal fixed-income choice compared to taxable bonds in a Roth account.

IRMAA: The Medicare Premium Surcharge

Medicare Part B and Part D premiums are income-based. Beneficiaries with MAGI (two years prior) above the base threshold pay higher premiums. These surcharges - called Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount (IRMAA) - stack on top of the Social Security taxation problem. For 2026, the IRMAA thresholds and surcharges are set based on 2024 income. The base threshold is approximately $106,000 (single) / $212,000 (MFJ).

A Roth conversion, capital gain realization, or large IRA withdrawal can push MAGI above an IRMAA tier, adding thousands in Medicare premium surcharges two years later. Managing income around these thresholds is a significant retirement tax planning consideration - especially in years with one-time large income events.
