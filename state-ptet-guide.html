State PTET Elections: Pass-Through Entity Tax & SALT Workaround

IRS Notice 2020-75  •  Entity-Level Deduction  •  36+ States  •  Resident Credit Issues  •  S-Corp & Partnership Mechanics  •  2026
IRC §164(b)(6) IRS Notice 2020-75 Updated 2026
← State & Compliance

The SALT deduction cap ($10,000 under TCJA, raised to $40,000 under OBBBA) effectively eliminated the federal deduction for state income taxes paid by high-income pass-through owners. States responded by enacting pass-through entity taxes - elective entity-level state taxes that the entity deducts as a business expense on its federal return, bypassing the individual SALT cap entirely. The IRS blessed this structure in Notice 2020-75. More than 36 states have now enacted PTET regimes, and PTET elections are among the most valuable routine tax elections available to S-corp and partnership owners in high-tax states.

The Mechanics in One Paragraph

Normally: entity earns $1M, pays no federal tax, passes income to owner who pays individual income tax - the state tax portion is capped at $40K on Schedule A. With PTET: entity elects to pay the state income tax at the entity level as a business expense, reducing federal QBI and ordinary income dollar for dollar with no cap. Owner receives a credit on their state return for the state tax paid by the entity. Net result: the owner effectively deducts their full state income tax on the federal return indirectly through the entity's business deduction. The federal tax savings at 37% on the uncapped deduction can be substantial.

The IRS Blessing: Notice 2020-75

IRS Notice 2020-75, issued November 9, 2020, confirmed that the IRS would not challenge state PTETs as violating the SALT cap. The notice stated that a partnership or S-corporation's payment of state and local income taxes imposed at the entity level is deductible by the entity in computing its non-separately stated income or loss. The notice was retroactive to TCJA's effective date (2018).

The IRS reasoning: the SALT cap under IRC §164(b)(6) limits the deduction for taxes "paid or accrued" by an "individual." A tax paid by an entity is not paid by an individual - it is a deductible business expense of the entity under IRC §164(a). The partners or shareholders then receive income that has already been reduced by the entity's state tax deduction, effectively getting the full deduction without being subject to the individual SALT cap.

How the Election Works: S-Corps vs. Partnerships

S-Corporations

The S-corp elects into the PTET regime by the state's deadline (varies by state - some require election by March 15 of the tax year, others allow annual elections). The S-corp pays state income tax at the entity level at a rate that approximates the owner's individual rate (varies by state). The payment is a deductible business expense of the S-corp, reducing income on Schedule K-1 to each shareholder. Each shareholder then claims a credit on their state individual income tax return for the state tax paid on their behalf by the S-corp.

Partnerships and Multi-Member LLCs

Same structure as S-corps. The partnership pays state tax at the entity level, deducts it as a business expense, and partners claim a resident credit. The mechanics are similar, but partnership PTET may interact differently with nonresident partner states - a nonresident partner may receive a credit in the entity's state but must separately file in their own state.

The Resident Credit Issue: Nonresident Partners

The most complex PTET situation involves entities with owners in multiple states. The entity pays state tax to State A (where the business operates). A partner who lives in State B gets a credit on their State B return for taxes paid to State A. But if State B does not recognize State A's PTET or if the credit rules are unfavorable, the partner may face double taxation - paying PTET in State A through the entity AND state income tax in State B without a full offsetting credit.

Multi-state PTET requires state-by-state analysis for each owner. Before making a PTET election in any state, map every owner's home state and analyze: (1) does the owner's home state have a PTET credit for taxes paid to other states? (2) Is the credit refundable or nonrefundable? (3) Is there a credit cap? An election that saves federal taxes may create a net negative result for a non-resident owner in an unfavorable credit state. Run the numbers for each partner individually.

OBBBA Context: SALT Cap Raised to $40,000

OBBBA P.L. 119-21 raised the SALT deduction cap from $10,000 to $40,000 (indexed for inflation, phasing out above $500,000 of adjusted gross income, reducing to $10,000 as AGI approaches $600,000). For owners with moderate state tax bills (under $40,000), the individual SALT cap may now be sufficient - reducing or eliminating the benefit of the PTET election for some taxpayers. For owners with state tax bills above $40,000 - which includes any high-income owner in NY, CA, or NJ - the PTET election remains highly valuable for the excess above the cap.
