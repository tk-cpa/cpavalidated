Subpart F & CFC Basics: What US Shareholders of Foreign Corporations Must Know

CFC Definition  •  Subpart F Income Categories  •  IRC §951 Inclusions  •  PTI Accounts  •  NCTI Post-OBBBA

← cpavalidated.com

If you own stock in a foreign corporation, you may be required to include certain income of that corporation in your own US taxable income - even if the corporation never paid you a dollar. This is Subpart F: a set of anti-deferral rules enacted in 1962 to prevent US shareholders from parking passive and mobile income offshore indefinitely. Understanding whether Subpart F applies, what income it reaches, and how TCJA and OBBBA changed the landscape is essential for any US person with foreign business interests.

Step One: Is the Foreign Corporation a CFC?

Subpart F only applies to Controlled Foreign Corporations (CFCs). A foreign corporation is a CFC if, on any day of the taxable year, more than 50% of its total combined voting power or total value is owned by "US shareholders." IRC §957(a).

Definition
US Shareholder
A US person who owns 10% or more of the total combined voting power or 10% or more of the total value of the foreign corporation's stock. IRC §951(b). Ownership is determined using attribution rules under IRC §958 - you are treated as owning stock held by related parties.
Control Test
More Than 50%
More than 50% of voting power or value must be owned by US shareholders (each owning 10%+). A foreign corporation owned 100% by one US person is a CFC. Ownership is tested on any single day of the tax year - even one day of CFC status triggers Subpart F for the full year.
TCJA dramatically expanded CFC status via downward attribution. Before 2018, downward attribution from foreign persons to US entities was limited. TCJA amended IRC §958(b) to apply IRC §318(a)(3) downward attribution rules without restriction. Many foreign subsidiaries that were not CFCs pre-2018 became CFCs overnight. Groups with partially foreign-owned structures should verify CFC status under post-TCJA rules.

Step Two: Does the CFC Have Subpart F Income?

CFC status is a prerequisite - but it does not automatically trigger an inclusion. The CFC must also earn Subpart F income. Subpart F captures income that is highly mobile (easily shifted to low-tax jurisdictions), passive, or derived from related-party transactions designed to move profits offshore. IRC §952.

The Major Categories

Foreign Personal Holding Company Income (FPHCI)
IRC §954(c)
The largest category in practice. Dividends, interest, rents, royalties, annuities, and gains from property. Also includes foreign currency gain and income equivalent to interest. Key exceptions: rents and royalties from active business operations received from unrelated parties are excluded. Related-CFC dividends and interest within the same country may qualify for the same-country exception under IRC §954(c)(6).
Foreign Base Company Sales Income (FBCSI)
IRC §954(d)
Income from buying from a related party and selling to anyone - or buying from anyone and selling to a related party - where the property is manufactured and used outside the CFC's country of incorporation. Classic example: a holding company in a zero-tax jurisdiction buys from a related manufacturer and sells to end customers, capturing the margin offshore. Exception: the CFC itself manufactures the property (or causes it to be manufactured through a contract manufacturer it substantially controls).
Foreign Base Company Services Income (FBCSvI)
IRC §954(e)
Income from services performed for or on behalf of a related party outside the CFC's country of incorporation. A CFC in the Cayman Islands that performs back-office services for a US parent has FBCSvI. Services performed entirely within the CFC's home country are excluded.
Insurance Income
IRC §953
Income from insuring or reinsuring US or related-party risks. Offshore captive insurance arrangements are a recurring IRS examination target under §953. The IRS has challenged structures where a US operating company pays premiums to an offshore captive owned by the same shareholders, particularly where the arrangement lacks economic substance.

De Minimis, Full-Inclusion, and High-Tax Exclusion

  • De minimis (IRC §954(b)(3)(A)): If total Subpart F income is less than the lesser of 5% of gross income or $1 million, the entire amount is treated as zero. No inclusion.
  • Full-inclusion (IRC §954(b)(3)(B)): If Subpart F income exceeds 70% of gross income, all of the CFC's gross income is treated as Subpart F income.
  • High-tax exclusion (IRC §954(b)(4)): Subpart F income taxed by a foreign country at an effective rate above 18.9% (90% x 21% US corporate rate) can be excluded by election - applied separately by category and requiring consistency.

Step Three: The IRC §951 Inclusion Mechanics

A US shareholder includes their pro-rata share of the CFC's Subpart F income in gross income for any year in which the foreign corporation is a CFC, computed as of the last day of the CFC's taxable year on which CFC status exists. IRC §951(a)(1)(A).

The inclusion is ordinary income - no preferential capital gains rates. For individual shareholders this is particularly costly: offshore passive income that would otherwise be qualified dividends (taxed at 20%) or long-term capital gains becomes ordinary income (up to 37%) under Subpart F.

The Previously Taxed Income (PTI) Account - IRC §959

Once Subpart F income is included in the US shareholder's gross income, it is tracked in a Previously Taxed Income (PTI) account. When the CFC later distributes that income, the distribution is excluded from gross income - no double taxation. IRC §959. Basis in the CFC stock is increased by the inclusion amount (IRC §961(a)) and decreased by excluded distributions (IRC §961(b)).

Accurate PTI records are essential. Errors in PTI tracking cause phantom income at distribution or missed exclusions. In M&A transactions, PTI accounts must be traced through acquisitions or errors compound across years.

Deemed-Paid Foreign Tax Credit

To prevent double taxation, a US shareholder can claim a deemed-paid foreign tax credit under IRC §960 for foreign income taxes paid by the CFC attributable to the included Subpart F income. The credit is subject to the §904 overall FTC limitation, applied by basket (passive, general, foreign branch). Individual shareholders claiming the §960 credit must include a gross-up under IRC §78 - the foreign taxes are added to income before the credit is taken.

TCJA (2017): GILTI Added on Top of Subpart F

The 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act added two major provisions alongside traditional Subpart F:

IRC §965 Transition Tax. A one-time deemed repatriation of post-1986 accumulated offshore earnings at reduced rates (15.5% on cash and cash equivalents, 8% on illiquid assets). Most taxpayers completed installment payments by 2025. New §965 issues arise primarily in M&A transactions involving companies with unrepatriated earnings built up pre-2018.

GILTI - IRC §951A. An annual minimum tax on the excess of the US shareholder's CFC tested income over a 10% deemed return on the CFC's tangible assets (QBAI). For C corporations, the §250 deduction reduced the effective GILTI rate to approximately 10.5% before FTCs (with a 20% FTC haircut reducing available credits). For individuals, GILTI was taxed at ordinary rates with no §250 deduction available directly - creating a planning mismatch that led many HNW shareholders to interpose S corporations or make §962 elections.

OBBBA (2025): GILTI Replaced by NCTI

OBBBA (P.L. 119-21, July 4, 2025) rewrote IRC §951A, renaming and restructuring GILTI as Net CFC Tested Income (NCTI). The key changes:

Key open items: individual §250 deduction mechanics, NCTI-Subpart F ordering, fiscal year taxpayer transition rules. Monitor irs.gov/newsroom and the Federal Register before finalizing 2026 CFC returns.

Form 5471: Annual Reporting and Penalties

US shareholders of CFCs must file Form 5471 annually. The form has five filer categories; CFC shareholders are typically Category 4 (US persons who control the CFC) or Category 5 (US shareholders of a CFC). Required schedules include the CFC balance sheet (Schedule F), income statement (Schedule C), earnings and profits (Schedule H), Subpart F computation (Schedule I), PTI tracking (Schedule J), tested income for NCTI (Schedule I-1), and related-party transactions (Schedule M).

Penalties and the open SOL trap. Failure to file Form 5471: $10,000 per form per year, with continuation penalties of $10,000 per 30-day period up to $50,000. IRC §6038(b). More critically: a missing Form 5471 leaves the statute of limitations on the entire tax return open indefinitely under IRC §6501(c)(8) - not just the international items. The IRS has used this provision to audit domestic items on returns with missing CFC filings years after the normal 3-year period closed.

Check-the-Box and Pre-Immigration Planning

A foreign corporation can elect to be treated as a disregarded entity or partnership under Treas. Reg. §301.7701-3 by filing Form 8832. This eliminates CFC status but triggers a deemed liquidation - potentially recognizing gain. The election is most useful when basis in the foreign corporation is close to or exceeds fair market value. For pre-immigration planning, check-the-box is one of several tools to restructure CFC exposure before US residency begins. See our Pre-Immigration Planning guide for a full analysis.
