Texas Franchise Tax: Margin Tax Guide

0.75% on Taxable Margin - $2.47M No-Tax-Due - COGS or Comp Deduction - Combined Reporting
TX Tax Code Sec. 171Texas ComptrollerForm 05-158
← State and Compliance

Texas has no corporate income tax and no personal income tax. But Texas does impose a franchise tax - the margin tax - on most entities doing business in the state. The margin tax is not an income tax. It is calculated on a modified gross revenue base with a choice of deductions, and a business can owe Texas franchise tax in a year it has a net loss. For most service companies, the effective rate is 0.75% of taxable margin. Understanding the margin tax is essential for any business operating in Texas.

Texas Franchise Tax 2026 Key Numbers

No-tax-due threshold: $2,470,000 in total revenue. Entities below this threshold owe no tax but must still file a no-tax-due report. This threshold is indexed periodically.

Standard rate: 0.75% of taxable margin for most entities.

Retail and wholesale rate: 0.375% for entities primarily engaged in retail or wholesale - half the standard rate.

EZ computation: 0.331% of total revenue (available for entities with less than $20 million revenue; avoids the margin deduction complexity entirely).

The Margin Tax Calculation: Four Deduction Options

Taxable margin equals total revenue minus the largest of four deductions. The taxpayer selects whichever produces the lowest taxable margin:

  • Cost of goods sold (COGS): Texas COGS defined under Texas Tax Code Sec. 171.1012 - narrower than federal COGS for goods; includes certain service delivery costs but excludes selling, general, and administrative expenses.
  • Compensation: Wages, salaries, and certain benefits paid to employees and officers. Limited to amounts paid to individuals, not entities.
  • 30% of total revenue: A flat percentage reduction available to all taxpayers regardless of cost structure.
  • $1,000,000: A flat dollar amount available to all taxpayers.

For most service businesses with limited goods but significant payroll, the compensation deduction is typically the largest. For product companies, COGS is often larger. The 30% of revenue option benefits high-margin businesses where neither COGS nor compensation exceeds 30% of revenue.

Combined Reporting for Affiliated Groups

Entities that are part of an affiliated group - generally, entities with more than 50% common ownership engaged in a unitary business - must file a combined Texas franchise tax report. The combined group is treated as a single entity: revenues and deductions are aggregated, and intercompany transactions are eliminated. This prevents income-shifting strategies that would otherwise reduce Texas franchise tax by locating revenue in out-of-state affiliates.

An out-of-state holding company may be pulled into the Texas combined group. If a Texas operating company and an out-of-state holding company are under common control and conduct a unitary business, the holding company may be included in the combined group even without Texas presence. The Texas Comptroller reviews combined group composition in audits and has taken expansive views of which entities constitute a unitary business.
